North Cascades National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
North Cascades National Park is located in the northern Cascade Range of Washington State and was established on October 2, 1968. It covers 789 square miles (2,042 sq km) and, together with the adjacent Ross Lake and Lake Chelan National Recreation Areas, forms the North Cascades National Park Complex covering over 1,000 square miles. The park contains some of the most rugged, dramatic mountain terrain in the contiguous United States — with over 300 glaciers (more than any other U.S. park outside Alaska), jagged peaks, turquoise glacial lakes, and impenetrable old-growth forest. It is one of the least visited parks in the Lower 48 and offers extraordinary wilderness solitude.

Location and Access:
North Cascades is located in northwestern Washington, approximately 120 miles northeast of Seattle. The primary access route is the North Cascades Highway (State Route 20), which traverses the park complex from Sedro-Woolley in the west to Winthrop in the east — one of the most scenic drives in the Pacific Northwest. The North Cascades Visitor Center is in Newhalem. The North Cascades Highway is typically closed from November through mid-April due to heavy snowfall. Seattle-Tacoma International Airport is the nearest major airport.

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for North Cascades National Park itself.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.
Note: Ross Lake National Recreation Area and Lake Chelan National Recreation Area, which adjoin the park, are fee-free as well. However, overnight backcountry permits require a small fee.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (July to September) is the primary season — trails are snow-free, the North Cascades Highway is fully open, and the alpine meadows are spectacular. July brings wildflowers at altitude. August and September offer the most stable weather. Temperatures are pleasant in the valleys (65-80°F) but cool at elevation. The park receives heavy precipitation — it averages 110 inches of rainfall annually. Fall colors in October are beautiful on the eastern slope around Winthrop. Spring brings snowmelt and waterfalls but most trails above 4,000 feet are snow-covered through June.

Top Attractions:
1. North Cascades Highway Scenic Drive: State Route 20 through the park complex is one of the great scenic highway drives in the Pacific Northwest, passing Gorge Creek Falls, Diablo Lake Overlook (turquoise glacial lake), Colonial Creek, and Washington Pass Overlook (with views of the jagged Liberty Bell massif). Allow 2-3 hours for the drive.
2. Diablo Lake: A strikingly turquoise glacial lake created by Diablo Dam on the Skagit River within Ross Lake NRA. The color comes from glacial flour suspended in the water. The 7.6-mile Diablo Lake Trail circles the lake. Rowboat and kayak rentals are available at the Diablo Lake Resort.
3. Cascade Pass Trail: The park's most iconic trail — a 7.4-mile round trip hike to a 5,392-foot mountain pass surrounded by glaciers and jagged peaks. The view from the pass, particularly at sunrise, is considered one of the most spectacular in the Cascades. Continue to Sahale Arm for even more dramatic alpine views.
4. Ross Lake: A 23-mile-long reservoir created by the massive Ross Dam, extending nearly to the Canadian border. Accessible primarily by boat or hiking trail (no road access to the lake itself), Ross Lake offers exceptional backcountry camping, kayaking, and fishing in a remote setting.
5. Stehekin: A remote community accessible only by ferry from Chelan or by hiking/flying in, located at the head of Lake Chelan within Lake Chelan NRA. A unique and charming community that operates without road access — the ferry journey from Chelan through the fjord-like lake is itself spectacular.
6. Thunder Creek Trail: A popular backcountry trail beginning at Colonial Creek Campground, following Thunder Creek deep into the mountains past old-growth trees, beaver ponds, and glacier views.

Wildlife:
North Cascades supports exceptional biodiversity. Gray wolves have returned to the park and a breeding pack has been documented. Grizzly bears (very rare and elusive) may inhabit the park's remote interior. Black bears, mountain goats, lynx, wolverines, cougars, and marmots are present. The Skagit River hosts large winter bald eagle concentrations feeding on spawning salmon. Over 300 bird species have been recorded.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Ladder Creek Falls Trail — 0.5 miles, easy, illuminated at night near Newhalem.
2. Sterling Munro Trail — 0.25 miles, easy, accessible overlook near visitor center.
3. River Loop Trail — 1.8 miles, easy, through old-growth forest near Newhalem.
4. Diablo Lake Trail — 7.6 miles round trip, moderate, around turquoise Diablo Lake.
5. Thunder Creek Trail — variable (up to 18 miles one way), moderate-strenuous, deep backcountry.
6. Cascade Pass Trail — 7.4 miles round trip, moderate-strenuous, to iconic mountain pass.
7. Sahale Arm Extension from Cascade Pass — additional 3.5 miles round trip, strenuous, into alpine glacier zone.

Camping:
Colonial Creek Campground (147 sites, north and south sections) near Diablo Lake is the largest. Goodell Creek Campground (21 sites) and Newhalem Creek Campground (111 sites) are near Newhalem. Backcountry camping requires a permit available at ranger stations. Ross Lake has numerous designated boat-in and hike-in campsites. Reservations for frontcountry campgrounds through recreation.gov.

Where to Stay:
The Ross Lake Resort, accessed only by boat or trail, is a unique floating cabin resort on Ross Lake — a memorable wilderness lodge experience. The Stehekin Valley Ranch and North Cascades Lodge at Stehekin provide lodging in the remote Stehekin community. Marblemount, Concrete, and Winthrop offer standard accommodations near the park.

Tips for Visitors:
- The North Cascades Highway closes in winter (typically November to April) — check road status before planning a visit.
- The Cascade Pass trailhead fills quickly on summer weekends — arrive before 8am or reserve a spot if a permit system is in effect.
- Weather changes rapidly in the Cascades — pack rain gear even on sunny days.
- The drive between the west and east sides of the highway reveals dramatically different ecosystems (wet rainforest to dry pine steppe) — drive the whole length.
- A Washington State Discover Pass is needed for parking at some trailheads — verify requirements before visiting.
- This is one of the most underrated and uncrowded national parks in the Lower 48 — a spectacular reward for those who make the effort.
