Hot Springs National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Hot Springs National Park is located in the Ouachita Mountains of central Arkansas and was established as a national park on March 4, 1921. It covers 8.7 square miles (22.5 sq km), making it one of the smallest national parks. The park protects 47 naturally flowing thermal springs that emerge from the western slope of Hot Springs Mountain at a constant temperature of 143°F (61.7°C). The springs have been drawing visitors for their alleged therapeutic properties for over a century. Hot Springs is unusual among national parks for its urban setting — the historic Bathhouse Row, a National Historic Landmark district featuring eight grand bathhouses built between 1892 and 1923, sits at the heart of the park on the main street of the city of Hot Springs.

Location and Access:
Hot Springs National Park is located within the city of Hot Springs, Arkansas, approximately 55 miles southwest of Little Rock via US-70 and US-270. Central Avenue (the main street of the city) runs through the heart of the park. Little Rock National Airport (LIT) is approximately 55 miles east. The park is open year-round and is easily accessible from the city's hotels and restaurants.

Entry Fees:
The park is free to enter. Bathhouse services (if the Quapaw Baths or Buckstaff are visited) have their own fees.

Best Time to Visit:
Spring (March to May) is beautiful — the dogwood trees bloom, temperatures are mild (60-75°F), and the Ouachita Mountains are lushly green. Fall (September to November) brings fall foliage to the mountain trails and comfortable hiking temperatures. Summer (June to August) is hot and humid (85-95°F) but popular — the trails are shaded by dense forest. Winter (December to February) is mild by national park standards (40-60°F) and the springs are active year-round.

Top Attractions:
1. Bathhouse Row: Eight magnificently restored bathhouses lining Central Avenue are the heart of the park experience. The Fordyce Bathhouse (1915) is now the park visitor center and has been beautifully restored to show its original grandeur — stained glass ceilings, marble interiors, and original hydrotherapy equipment. The Buckstaff Bathhouse and Quapaw Baths are still operating as working bathhouses.
2. Bathing in the Thermal Waters: The Buckstaff Bathhouse offers traditional thermal baths including the original soaking tubs and a hot pack service — one of the few original bathhouse traditions still operating since 1912. The Quapaw Baths & Spa offers more modern spa treatments using the thermal water. A unique experience that connects visitors to the park's century-old history.
3. Hot Springs Mountain Tower: A 216-foot tower at the summit of Hot Springs Mountain (1,256 feet) offering panoramic views of the Ouachita Mountains and the city of Hot Springs. Reached by car or the Sunset Trail.
4. Fordyce Bathhouse Visitor Center: Even if not bathing, touring the Fordyce is worthwhile — it's one of the best-preserved examples of Gilded Age resort architecture in America, with elaborate tile work, stained glass, and original equipment.
5. Thermal Fountains and Jug Springs: Several spots along Central Avenue where naturally hot spring water flows into public jug fountains — visitors can fill containers with hot spring water (which is safe to drink after cooling). A living tradition of the spring culture.
6. Mountain Trail System: Over 26 miles of trails wind through the surrounding Ouachita Mountains offering forested hiking with views. The West Mountain Summit Trail, Sunset Trail, and Peak Trail network offer the best experiences.

Wildlife:
The park's forested mountain slopes support white-tailed deer, wild turkey, red foxes, opossums, raccoons, and over 100 bird species including red-tailed hawks, barred owls, and various woodpeckers. The streams support native fish species.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Hot Springs Mountain Trail — 4 miles loop, easy-moderate, to the tower.
2. Gulley Trail — 2 miles round trip, easy, through forested gulley.
3. West Mountain Summit Trail — 3.5 miles loop, moderate.
4. Sunset Trail — 10 miles one way, moderate, traverse of multiple mountains.
5. Goat Rock Trail — 0.8 miles round trip, easy, rocky outcrop viewpoint.

Camping:
There is no campground in the national park itself. Gulley Park and Lake Catherine State Park nearby provide camping options. The city of Hot Springs and Garvan Woodland Gardens area have private RV campgrounds.

Where to Stay:
The park is entirely within an active city with dozens of hotel options along US-270 and Central Avenue. The historic downtown area near Bathhouse Row has several charming hotels including the Arlington Resort Hotel & Spa (historic grand resort) and the Majestic Hotel. Hot Springs is a vibrant resort city with restaurants, casinos (Oaklawn Racing Casino Resort), and entertainment.

Tips for Visitors:
- Take a bath at the Buckstaff or Quapaw — it's the most authentic way to experience what made Hot Springs famous.
- The Fordyce Bathhouse Visitor Center (free to enter and tour) is one of the most elegant visitor centers in the national park system.
- Download the NPS park app for a self-guided audio tour of Bathhouse Row.
- Fill a jug or bottle at one of the thermal fountain spigots along Central Avenue — a free, unique souvenir.
- The park is perfectly combined with a broader Hot Springs city visit: restaurants, the arts district, Lake Hamilton, and Oaklawn.
- This is a wonderfully accessible park for all ages and abilities — the main attractions require minimal physical exertion.
- The thermal springs emerge at 143°F — never touch or enter the water at source.
