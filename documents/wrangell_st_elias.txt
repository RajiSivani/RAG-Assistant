Wrangell-St. Elias National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Wrangell-St. Elias National Park and Preserve is located in southeastern Alaska and was established on December 2, 1980. It is the largest national park in the United States by a significant margin, covering approximately 20,587 square miles (53,321 sq km) — larger than the entire country of Switzerland and six times the size of Yellowstone. The park encompasses four major mountain ranges (the Wrangell, St. Elias, Alaska, and Chugach ranges), the continent's largest non-polar icefield, and more peaks above 14,500 feet than anywhere else in North America. Mount St. Elias (18,008 feet) is the second-highest peak in the United States. The park protects a vast wilderness of glaciers, volcanoes, wild rivers, and boreal forest. In 1979, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site jointly with Canada's Kluane National Park. Despite its immense size, Wrangell-St. Elias receives relatively few visitors, making it one of the wildest and most remote destinations in the national park system.

Location and Access:
Wrangell-St. Elias is located in southeastern Alaska, approximately 200 miles east of Anchorage. The two primary access roads are the Nabesna Road (45 miles of gravel, entering the park from the north via Slana off the Tok Cutoff/AK-1) and the McCarthy Road (60 miles of gravel, entering from the west via Chitina). The nearest major city is Anchorage, approximately 200 miles to the west. Fairbanks is approximately 300 miles to the northwest. The primary visitor hub is the historic mining town of McCarthy and the adjacent mining camp of Kennecott, accessible from the end of the McCarthy Road. There is no road access to the majority of the park — most of the park's interior is accessible only by small aircraft or on foot. Regional air taxis operate from Chitina, McCarthy, Gulkana, and Glennallen.

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for Wrangell-St. Elias National Park and Preserve.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to all national parks.
Note: Privately operated facilities within the park (including lodging, guided tours, and the Kennecott Mines historic buildings) charge their own fees.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (June to August) is the primary visitor season and the only practical time for most visitors. June and July offer the longest daylight hours (near 24-hour daylight in June), the best road conditions, and peak wildflower blooms. August brings abundant blueberries and the first hints of fall color. Temperatures in the McCarthy/Kennecott area range from 50-75°F in summer. The McCarthy Road typically opens in late May and closes or becomes hazardous in October. Shoulder seasons (late May and September) offer fewer crowds, dramatic light, and wildlife activity — September brings spectacular fall foliage and active moose and bears preparing for winter. Winter is extremely remote and harsh — only experienced wilderness travelers with extensive cold-weather skills should attempt winter visits, and the McCarthy Road is typically impassable.

Top Attractions:
1. Kennecott Mines National Historic Landmark: The crown jewel of the park's cultural heritage — a remarkably preserved early 20th-century copper mining complex perched on the edge of Kennecott Glacier. The iconic red buildings include concentrators, a power plant, hospital, and worker housing, all largely intact. The Kennecott Mines Company operated here from 1903 to 1938, extracting over $200 million in copper ore. Guided tours of the mill buildings are operated by St. Elias Alpine Guides — highly recommended. The site is a 5-mile walk, bike, or shuttle ride from McCarthy.
2. Root Glacier Trail: The most accessible glacier hike in the park — a 4-mile round trip trail from Kennecott across the rocky moraines to the surface of Root Glacier, a tributary of the massive Kennecott Glacier. Walking on the glacier surface (with crampons, available from guide services) offers an extraordinary experience of crevasses, moulins, and blue ice. The view of the Wrangell Mountains from the glacier is spectacular.
3. McCarthy: The quirky, remote gateway town to the park — a genuine Alaskan frontier community accessible only via the McCarthy Road footbridge over the Kennicott River (vehicles park at the road's end). McCarthy has a handful of lodges, restaurants, and outfitters. The town's independent spirit and wilderness setting make it one of Alaska's most distinctive destinations.
4. Wrangell Mountains Views: The Wrangell Mountains include four active or potentially active volcanoes — Mount Wrangell (14,163 feet), Mount Drum (12,010 feet), Mount Sanford (16,237 feet), and Mount Blackburn (16,390 feet). Views of these immense volcanic peaks from the Tok Cutoff highway or Nabesna Road on clear days are among the most dramatic mountain panoramas in North America.
5. Nabesna Road: A 45-mile gravel road penetrating the northern section of the park through boreal forest, wetlands, and open tundra with views of the Wrangell Mountains. The road offers excellent wildlife viewing (moose, caribou, wolves, bears) and accesses remote trailheads. A high-clearance vehicle is recommended; road conditions vary and some stream crossings can be impassable after heavy rain.
6. Glacier Flightseeing: The park contains approximately 60% of Alaska's glacial area, including the Bagley Icefield — the largest subpolar icefield in North America — and the massive Nabesna, Kennecott, and Chitina glaciers. Flightseeing tours from McCarthy, Chitina, or Gulkana offer access to sights utterly inaccessible by land — vast icefields, glacial valleys, and summit views of the St. Elias Mountains. This is the single most spectacular way to experience the park's scale.

Wildlife:
Wrangell-St. Elias supports abundant and diverse wildlife across its vast wilderness. Moose are the most commonly seen large mammal, found throughout the lowland forests and river corridors. Grizzly bears and black bears are common throughout the park — bear encounters are a realistic possibility on any backcountry trip. Wolves and coyotes range across the park's interior. Dall sheep are frequently seen on rocky slopes and ridgelines near Kennecott and throughout the Wrangell Mountains. Caribou herds migrate through the park's northern sections. Wolverines, lynx, beavers, and river otters inhabit the lowland areas. The park's rivers and lakes support salmon runs (all five Pacific salmon species are present), attracting eagles, bears, and other predators. Over 200 bird species have been recorded, including golden eagles, peregrine falcons, trumpeter swans, and numerous shorebirds during migration.

Top Hiking Trails:
Near Kennecott/McCarthy:
1. Root Glacier Trail — 4 miles round trip, easy-moderate, to the edge of Root Glacier with stunning Wrangell Mountain views. The most popular trail in the park.
2. Bonanza Mine Trail — 8.6 miles round trip, strenuous, climbing 3,800 feet to historic Bonanza Mine on the ridge above Kennecott with panoramic views of Kennecott Glacier and the Wrangell Mountains. One of the best day hikes in the park.
3. Erie Mine Trail — 6.6 miles round trip, strenuous, to another historic mine above Kennecott with excellent views.
4. Jumbo Mine Trail — 5.8 miles round trip, strenuous, through alpine terrain to ore tram equipment and historic mine workings.

Nabesna Road Area:
5. Skookum Volcano Trail — 9 miles round trip, strenuous, to the rim of a cinder cone volcano with views of the Wrangell volcanic field.
6. Twin Lakes Trail — 2 miles round trip, easy, to scenic subalpine lakes with mountain views.

Backcountry:
The vast majority of the park has no maintained trails — experienced wilderness travelers navigate by map and GPS across open tundra, river bars, and glacial terrain. The park's backcountry is among the most demanding and remote in the national park system. Backcountry travel requires strong wilderness skills, bear-resistant food containers, and comprehensive preparation.

Camping:
There are no developed campgrounds within the main park (McCarthy/Kennecott area). Dispersed backcountry camping is permitted throughout the park with no permit required (leave no trace principles apply). Near the McCarthy Road terminus, several private campgrounds and camping areas operate in and around McCarthy. Campgrounds with basic facilities exist along the Nabesna Road at Kendesnii (operated by the NPS) and at private sites. Car camping is common along the McCarthy Road at pullouts and designated areas. Bear canisters are strongly recommended throughout the park.

Where to Stay:
McCarthy and Kennecott offer limited but characterful lodging options, including the historic Kennecott Glacier Lodge (operated within Kennecott) and several smaller lodges and B&Bs in McCarthy — book many months in advance for summer. Chitina, at the start of the McCarthy Road, has limited services. Glennallen (approximately 60 miles west of Chitina on the Richardson Highway) is the primary supply town for the region with motels, gas, groceries, and services. For the Nabesna Road area, Slana and Tok have basic accommodations. Many visitors base themselves in Glennallen and make day trips or arrange fly-in adventures.

Tips for Visitors:
- The McCarthy Road is unpaved gravel for its entire 60-mile length and notorious for flat tires — carry at least two spare tires and be prepared for a slow, bumpy drive of 2-3 hours each way. Do not drive at night.
- Glacier travel requires crampons and ideally instruction — St. Elias Alpine Guides and other local guide services offer guided glacier hikes, ice climbing, and mountaineering trips. Booking a guided glacier walk is strongly recommended for first-timers.
- Bears are common throughout the park — carry bear spray, know how to use it, and store food properly. The park issues no permits for backcountry travel, but the visitor center staff can provide current bear activity reports and route advice.
- Book the Kennecott mill building tour in advance — tours fill up during the peak summer season and offer the best interpretation of the mining history.
- Flightseeing is expensive but worth it — the park's scale and its glaciers and mountains are incomprehensible from the ground alone. Even a 30-minute flight dramatically changes the visitor's understanding of the landscape.
- The park has extremely limited cell service — download offline maps (Gaia GPS or CalTopo) before your trip and file a trip plan with the park or a responsible contact.
- Fuel and supplies are scarce — fill up in Glennallen before driving the McCarthy Road. Carry extra food and water.
- Weather is highly unpredictable and can change rapidly — always be prepared for cold, rain, and wind regardless of the forecast, especially in the mountains and on glaciers.
- The Wrangell-St. Elias Visitor Center in Copper Center (on the Richardson Highway) is an excellent first stop — excellent exhibits, friendly rangers, and helpful trip planning resources.
- Mosquitoes and other biting insects can be extremely numerous in June and July — bring head nets and DEET-based repellent.
