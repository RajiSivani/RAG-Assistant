Isle Royale National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Isle Royale National Park consists of an isolated archipelago in the northwest corner of Lake Superior and was established as a national park on April 3, 1940. It covers 893 square miles (2,314 sq km), the majority of which is water. The main island, Isle Royale, is 45 miles long and up to 9 miles wide — the largest island in Lake Superior. The park is one of the least-visited national parks in the lower 48, receiving only about 25,000 visitors per year, partly because it is accessible only by boat or seaplane and is closed from November through mid-April. Isle Royale is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve and is famous for its long-running scientific study of the wolf-moose predator-prey relationship — the longest such continuous wildlife study in the world, conducted since 1958. The island offers exceptional wilderness hiking, kayaking, scuba diving on shipwrecks, and near-total solitude.

Location and Access:
Isle Royale is located in Lake Superior, 15 miles from the Michigan shore and 22 miles from the Minnesota shore. The island has no road connections to the mainland. Ferries operate from Houghton and Copper Harbor, Michigan, and Grand Portage, Minnesota. The crossing takes 2-6 hours depending on the departure point. Isle Royale Seaplanes provides air service from Houghton. The nearest airport is Houghton County Memorial Airport (HOU) in Michigan.

Entry Fees:
Park entrance: $7 per person per day (included in ferry ticket from most operators).
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 (covers daily fees).
Note: The park is closed November through mid-April.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (mid-June to August) is peak season — all ferry services operate, the weather is at its best, and wildlife activity is high. Blueberries ripen in August. Moose are commonly seen. Wildflowers bloom in late June and July. Fall (September to mid-October) is spectacular for solitude — crowds drop dramatically, fall color appears, and moose and wolves become more active. Early September is arguably the best week of the year. Mosquitoes and blackflies are worst in June; by August they are much reduced.

Top Attractions:
1. Moose Viewing: Isle Royale has one of the highest densities of moose in North America — approximately 1,500 moose on an island the size of a small county. Encountering a bull moose at the edge of a lake while paddling or on a trail is a quintessential Isle Royale experience. Tobin Harbor, Siskiwit Bay, and wetland areas near the trail system are productive.
2. Rock Harbor Area: The main visitor hub on the east end of the island — ferry dock, visitor center, lodge, restaurant, camp store, and marina. Day hike options fan out from here and include the Scoville Point Loop and Sugarloaf Mountain.
3. Greenstone Ridge Trail: The 40-mile spine trail running the entire length of the island — the most ambitious multi-day hiking route in the park, taking 4-6 days to traverse. Panoramic views of the island and Lake Superior from the high ridges.
4. Windigo: The visitor hub on the west end of the island (served by Grand Portage ferry) — quieter than Rock Harbor. An excellent starting or ending point for the Greenstone Ridge traverse.
5. Scuba Diving the Shipwrecks: Lake Superior's cold, clear water preserves historic shipwrecks in extraordinary condition. The park has 10 diveable wrecks ranging from 19th-century schooners to 20th-century steamships. The America (near Washington Harbor) and Kamloops are famous dive sites — however, the wrecks are deep and cold; advanced certification and dry suits are required.
6. Sea Kayaking: Paddling among the outer islands, through inland waterways, and along the Lake Superior shore is one of the park's finest experiences. The Passage Island, McCargoe Cove, and Five Finger Bay areas are highlights. Conditions can be challenging — Lake Superior is dangerous for inexperienced paddlers.

Wildlife:
Isle Royale's wolf-moose dynamic is the most studied predator-prey relationship in the world. The wolf population fluctuates and has been supplemented by new wolves relocated to the island in recent years to strengthen the genetically limited population. The park has no bears or deer — the moose and wolves exist in unusual ecological simplicity. Beavers, river otters, red foxes, loons (very common on the lakes), bald eagles, and osprey are all present. The surrounding Lake Superior waters have lake trout, coho salmon, and other cold-water fish species.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Scoville Point Loop — 4 miles loop, easy-moderate, rocky shoreline at island's eastern tip.
2. Sugarloaf Mountain — 3.8 miles round trip, moderate, panoramic views near Rock Harbor.
3. Lookout Louise — 4 miles round trip, moderate, elevated view over Tobin Harbor.
4. Mount Ojibway Tower — 7.6 miles round trip (from Rock Harbor), moderate.
5. Greenstone Ridge Trail — 40 miles one way, strenuous multi-day, island traverse.
6. Minong Ridge Trail — 26 miles one way, strenuous, more remote and rugged than Greenstone.

Camping:
Backcountry camping is at designated sites throughout the island — many accessible by trail, others by water. Sites feature tent pads and pit toilets. A camping quota system applies — register at Rock Harbor or Windigo when you arrive. Rock Harbor Lodge and Windigo Camper Cabins provide basic cabin-style accommodations with some services.

Where to Stay:
Rock Harbor Lodge is the only in-park lodge — offering rooms, a dining room (excellent freshwater fish), and canoe/kayak rentals. Book many months in advance. Most visitors camp in the backcountry.

Tips for Visitors:
- Plan and reserve ferry passage many months in advance — ferries fill up for summer weekends.
- The park is remote and rescue is far away — prepare for genuine wilderness travel.
- Carry a water filter — natural water sources are abundant but require treatment.
- Moose are commonly seen but are large and unpredictable — give them space.
- Lake Superior weather changes rapidly — always have rain gear and extra warm layers, even in summer.
- The park offers true solitude unlike any in the lower 48 — once you arrive, leave the outside world behind.
- Wolves are rarely seen but occasionally spotted at dawn and dusk along the Greenstone Ridge.
