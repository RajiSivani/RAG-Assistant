Joshua Tree National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Joshua Tree National Park is located in southeastern California and was established as a national park on October 31, 1994. It covers 1,235 square miles (3,199 sq km) and sits at the junction of two distinct desert ecosystems — the higher, cooler, wetter Mojave Desert in the west (where the iconic Joshua trees grow) and the lower, hotter, drier Colorado Desert in the east. The park is world-famous for its unique desert landscapes of twisted Joshua trees, massive granite boulder formations, and vast open skies. It is one of the premier rock climbing destinations in the United States with over 8,000 climbing routes, and one of the best stargazing destinations in Southern California.

Location and Access:
The park is located approximately 130 miles east of Los Angeles and 175 miles northeast of San Diego. The main north entrance is near the town of Joshua Tree on Highway 62 (Twentynine Palms Highway). The west entrance is near the town of Joshua Tree as well. The south entrance at Cottonwood Spring is accessed from Interstate 10. Palm Springs Regional Airport (45 miles) and Los Angeles International Airport (130 miles) are the nearest commercial airports. The park is a popular weekend destination from Los Angeles and San Diego.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $30 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $25, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $15, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Fall and Spring (October to April) are the ideal seasons — temperatures are mild and comfortable (60-80°F during the day). Spring (March to May) brings spectacular wildflower blooms in wet years, covering the desert floor in yellow, purple, and orange. Winters are cool with occasional frost at night. Summer (June to September) is extremely hot — daytime temperatures regularly exceed 105°F in the lower Colorado Desert. If visiting in summer, limit hiking to before 8am and after 5pm, carry extraordinary amounts of water, and never hike alone. The park is a year-round destination with proper preparation.

Top Attractions:
1. Joshua Trees: The park's namesake trees — a species of yucca (Yucca brevifolia) that grows only in the Mojave Desert at elevations between 2,000 and 6,000 feet. Their twisted, spiky silhouettes against the sunset sky are iconic. The highest concentration is in the Queen Valley and Lost Horse Valley areas.
2. Skull Rock Nature Trail: A 1.7-mile loop trail through one of the park's most dramatic boulder formations — including Skull Rock, a large boulder naturally eroded into the shape of a skull. An excellent introduction to Joshua Tree's geology.
3. Cholla Cactus Garden: A flat 0.25-mile loop through a dense forest of teddy bear cholla cactus — beautiful in the early morning light but extremely hazardous to touch. The spines detach easily and are very difficult to remove from skin.
4. Keys View: A panoramic overlook at 5,185 feet offering views across the Coachella Valley, the Salton Sea, and on clear days all the way to Mexico. At night it offers stunning city light views.
5. Barker Dam: A short 1.1-mile loop to a small reservoir built in the early 1900s by ranchers. The dam is frequently visited by bighorn sheep and other wildlife.
6. Hidden Valley: A 1-mile loop trail through a rock-enclosed valley once used as a cattle rustlers' hideout. Surrounded by massive granite boulders and one of the best rock climbing areas in the park.
7. Cottonwood Spring Oasis: In the southern Colorado Desert section of the park, this natural spring creates a lush oasis of cottonwood and fan palm trees — an important water source for wildlife and a great birding spot.
8. Rock Climbing Areas: Joshua Tree is one of the top rock climbing destinations in North America. Cap Rock, Hidden Valley, and Jumbo Rocks offer thousands of routes from beginner to expert. Gear rentals and guided lessons available in the town of Joshua Tree.

Wildlife:
Joshua Tree's two desert ecosystems support surprising biodiversity. Animals include desert bighorn sheep (commonly seen near Barker Dam), coyotes, kit foxes, jackrabbits, ground squirrels, roadrunners, Gambel's quail, golden eagles, peregrine falcons, Costa's hummingbirds, and over 250 bird species. The desert tortoise (threatened species) is present but rarely seen. Chuckwalla lizards, rattlesnakes (5 species), and tarantulas are also found in the park. Wildlife is most active at dawn and dusk.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Skull Rock Nature Trail — 1.7 miles round trip, easy, great geology.
2. Barker Dam — 1.1 miles round trip, easy, wildlife and history.
3. Hidden Valley Trail — 1 mile round trip, easy, rock formations.
4. Boy Scout Trail — 7.8 miles one way, moderate, excellent backcountry.
5. Lost Horse Mine Trail — 4 miles round trip, moderate, historic gold mine.
6. Ryan Mountain — 3 miles round trip, strenuous, 360-degree park views from summit.
7. Mastodon Peak — 3 miles round trip, moderate, views and old mining site.
8. 49 Palms Oasis — 3 miles round trip, moderate, fan palm oasis.

Stargazing:
Joshua Tree is designated an International Dark Sky Park and is one of the best stargazing locations in Southern California due to its distance from major city lights. The Milky Way is clearly visible on moonless nights from spring through fall. Popular stargazing spots include Skull Rock parking area, Cholla Cactus Garden, and Cottonwood Spring. The park hosts an annual Night Sky Festival each spring.

Camping:
The park has nine campgrounds. Jumbo Rocks (124 sites) is the largest and most iconic campground, surrounded by massive boulders. Belle, White Tank, and Ryan Campgrounds are smaller. Indian Cove and Black Rock require reservations at recreation.gov. Backcountry camping is allowed in designated areas 1 mile from roads.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodging. The gateway towns of Joshua Tree and Twentynine Palms (north side) and Indio/Palm Desert (south side, 40 miles) offer hotels and vacation rentals. The town of Joshua Tree has a funky arts community with numerous unique vacation rentals and small inns. Palm Springs (45 miles west) is a popular upscale resort town base.

Tips for Visitors:
- Carry at least 4 liters of water per person per day — more in summer. People die of dehydration in this park every year.
- Never touch cholla cactus — use two sticks to remove detached segments from clothing or skin.
- Watch where you step — rattlesnakes blend perfectly with rocks and sand.
- Sunset and sunrise photography is extraordinary — plan to be at Keys View or among the Joshua trees at these times.
- The park is much larger than it looks — the drive from the north entrance to Cottonwood Spring is 45 miles.
- Cell service is non-existent throughout most of the park — download offline maps and file a trip plan.
