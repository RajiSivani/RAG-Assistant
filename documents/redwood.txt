Redwood National and State Parks - Complete Guide

Overview:
Redwood National and State Parks is a combined area of cooperatively managed federal and state parklands located in northwestern California, established as a national park on October 2, 1968. The combined national and state parks cover 139 square miles (360 sq km) and protect nearly half of all remaining old-growth coast redwood forest. The coast redwood (Sequoia sempervirens) is the tallest living tree species on Earth — trees in the park regularly exceed 350 feet, and Hyperion, discovered in the park in 2006, is the world's tallest known living tree at 380.3 feet. The parks are a UNESCO World Heritage Site and International Biosphere Reserve.

Location and Access:
Redwood National and State Parks stretch along approximately 50 miles of the Northern California coast in Humboldt and Del Norte Counties, with the main communities of Orick, Crescent City, and Klamath serving as gateways. The parks are accessed via U.S. Highway 101 (the Pacific Coast Highway). The nearest major airports are Arcata-Eureka Airport (40 miles south of Orick) and Rogue Valley International-Medford Airport in Oregon (90 miles north of Crescent City). San Francisco is approximately 330 miles to the south.

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for Redwood National Park itself.
The adjacent state parks (Prairie Creek Redwoods, Del Norte Coast Redwoods, and Jedediah Smith Redwoods) charge a day-use fee: $8-12 per vehicle for California State Parks.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to national park areas (not state park areas).

Best Time to Visit:
The parks are open year-round. Summer (June to August) is the most popular time — fog can shroud the coast in the mornings (the famous "June Gloom") but typically clears by afternoon. Temperatures are mild (55-70°F). Spring (April to June) brings lush green vegetation and excellent wildflowers. Fall (September to October) is outstanding — clear weather, golden light filtering through the redwoods, and elk rut in September-October. Winter (November to March) is rainy and cool (45-55°F) but the forest feels primeval and uncrowded. The old-growth forest is beautiful in any season.

Top Attractions:
1. Tall Trees Grove: Home to some of the world's tallest trees, accessible via a 4-mile round trip trail requiring a free permit. The grove provides an immersive old-growth experience with trees exceeding 350 feet. Permits are available at the Thomas H. Kuchel Visitor Center near Orick.
2. Fern Canyon: An extraordinary slot canyon in Prairie Creek Redwoods State Park where five-finger ferns blanket vertical canyon walls up to 50 feet high above a small stream. Featured in Jurassic Park 2 and other films. The 1-mile loop is one of the most magical natural places in California.
3. Lady Bird Johnson Grove: A beautiful grove of old-growth redwoods accessible via a 1.5-mile loop trail — dedicated to Lady Bird Johnson in 1969. One of the most accessible and impressive grove experiences in the park.
4. Newton B. Drury Scenic Parkway: A 10-mile scenic road through Prairie Creek Redwoods State Park passing through magnificent old-growth redwood forest. Walking, cycling, and driving are all superb. The parkway provides access to the Big Tree and other popular trailheads.
5. Gold Bluffs Beach: A wild, undeveloped beach below grass-covered gold bluffs, accessible near Fern Canyon. Roosevelt elk are commonly seen grazing in the meadows above the beach. One of the most dramatic and remote beaches on the California coast.
6. Jedediah Smith Redwoods: The northernmost unit of the parks, featuring Howland Hill Road — a narrow, unpaved road through some of the most impressive old-growth forest in the entire park complex. The drive (or hike) through this forest is awe-inspiring.

Wildlife:
Roosevelt elk are the signature wildlife species — large herds of 50+ animals are commonly seen in Elk Prairie near Prairie Creek Redwoods and at Gold Bluffs Beach. Black bears, mountain lions, bobcats, river otters, and gray foxes inhabit the parks. The marine environment offshore supports gray whale migration (January-March and March-June), harbor seals, Steller sea lions, and abundant seabirds. Over 370 bird species have been recorded including marbled murrelets (threatened) that nest in old-growth redwoods.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Fern Canyon Loop — 0.9 miles, moderate, through the fern-covered canyon walls.
2. Lady Bird Johnson Grove Loop — 1.5 miles, easy, beautiful old-growth loop.
3. Tall Trees Trail — 4 miles round trip, moderate, to some of the world's tallest trees (permit required).
4. James Irvine Trail to Fern Canyon — 8.6 miles round trip, moderate, through diverse forest.
5. Rhododendron Trail — 6.2 miles round trip, moderate, spectacular when rhododendrons bloom in May.
6. Boy Scout Tree Trail — 5.6 miles round trip, moderate, to one of the largest redwoods.
7. Lost Man Creek Trail — 2+ miles, easy, along a beautiful redwood-lined creek.

Camping:
Elk Prairie Campground (75 sites) in Prairie Creek Redwoods and Gold Bluffs Beach Campground (26 sites, reservable) are the most scenic. Mill Creek Campground in Del Norte Coast Redwoods and Jedediah Smith Campground (108 sites) are in stunning redwood settings. All campgrounds reserve through ReserveCaliforna.com. Backcountry camping with a free permit is available at Redwood Creek.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodge. Crescent City and Eureka offer the widest range of accommodations. The communities of Orick, Klamath, and Trinidad offer smaller lodging options closer to specific park areas. The Elk Meadow Cabins near Orick are a popular and convenient option.

Tips for Visitors:
- Obtain the Tall Trees Grove permit first thing in the morning at the Kuchel Visitor Center — a limited number are issued daily.
- Visit Fern Canyon at low water (summer/fall) for the best experience — high water in winter and spring can flood the canyon.
- Howland Hill Road in Jedediah Smith requires a high-clearance vehicle (it is a narrow unpaved road) but the forest drive is extraordinary.
- Roosevelt elk viewing is best at Elk Prairie at dawn and dusk.
- Check road conditions in winter — coastal California storms can temporarily close or damage roads.
- The parks are large and spread over 50 miles — plan your itinerary carefully and stay multiple nights to see multiple units.
- Wear layers even in summer — coastal temperatures are cool and fog is common.
