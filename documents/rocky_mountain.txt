Rocky Mountain National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Rocky Mountain National Park is located in north-central Colorado and was established on January 26, 1915. It covers 415 square miles (1,076 sq km) and sits at elevations ranging from 7,860 feet to 14,259 feet above sea level. The park contains 77 mountain peaks above 12,000 feet and 14 peaks exceeding 13,000 feet, including Longs Peak at 14,259 feet — the park's highest point and one of Colorado's famous "fourteeners." The Continental Divide runs through the center of the park, dividing the Pacific and Atlantic watersheds.

Location and Access:
The park is located approximately 65 miles northwest of Denver, Colorado. The two main entrance areas are Estes Park on the east side and Grand Lake on the west side. The most popular entrance is the Beaver Meadows Entrance near Estes Park. Denver International Airport is the nearest major airport (90 miles from Estes Park). A timed entry permit reservation system is in place from May through October — check the park website before visiting.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.
Note: A timed entry permit ($2 reservation fee) is required from late May through mid-October for vehicles entering the park between 5am and 6pm. These must be reserved in advance at recreation.gov.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (June to August) is peak season — Trail Ridge Road is fully open, wildflowers bloom in alpine meadows, temperatures are mild at lower elevations (65-80°F) though cold at altitude. Afternoon thunderstorms are common at high elevations. Fall (September to October) is spectacular for elk rut — the park hosts one of the largest elk herds in North America and the bugling of bulls echoes through the valleys. Aspen groves turn golden. Spring (April to May) brings snowmelt, waterfalls, and newborn wildlife but Trail Ridge Road remains closed. Winter (November to March) offers snowshoeing and cross-country skiing on the east side, with Trail Ridge Road closed above Many Parks Curve.

Top Attractions:
1. Trail Ridge Road: America's highest paved through-road, reaching 12,183 feet at its highest point. This 48-mile road crosses the Continental Divide and passes through arctic tundra, offering spectacular views. Open approximately June through October depending on snowfall.
2. Bear Lake: A stunning glacially-carved alpine lake surrounded by peaks, easily accessible and the starting point for many popular trails. Very crowded in summer — arrive before 8am or take the free shuttle.
3. Longs Peak: Colorado's northernmost fourteener at 14,259 feet. The Keyhole Route is a 14.5-mile round trip, extremely strenuous technical climb requiring an early pre-dawn start (typically 3-4am) and some scrambling. No technical climbing equipment needed but experience essential.
4. Elk Meadow / Moraine Park: Two large open meadows that are the best places to observe the park's elk herd — particularly spectacular during fall rut (September-October) when bulls bugle and spar.
5. Alberta Falls: A beautiful 30-foot waterfall reached by a moderate 1.6-mile round trip trail from the Glacier Gorge trailhead near Bear Lake. One of the most popular short hikes in the park.
6. Alpine Visitor Center: Located at 11,796 feet on Trail Ridge Road, this is the highest visitor center in the National Park System. The short Tundra Communities Trail here shows the fragile alpine ecosystem.
7. Kawuneeche Valley: On the quieter west side of the park, this broad valley follows the headwaters of the Colorado River and offers excellent moose and elk viewing.

Wildlife:
Rocky Mountain National Park supports over 60 mammal species. The park's elk herd numbers approximately 600-800 animals and is one of the most visible wildlife experiences in the national park system. Other animals include mule deer, moose (west side), bighorn sheep, black bears, mountain lions, coyotes, bobcats, pikas (high alpine), and marmots. Over 280 bird species have been recorded including ptarmigan, Clark's nutcrackers, and Steller's jays.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Alberta Falls — 1.6 miles round trip, moderate.
2. Bear Lake Loop — 0.8 miles round trip, easy, accessible.
3. Emerald Lake via Dream and Nymph Lakes — 3.6 miles round trip, moderate.
4. Sky Pond — 9 miles round trip, strenuous, stunning alpine lake.
5. Flattop Mountain — 8.8 miles round trip, strenuous, crosses Continental Divide.
6. Longs Peak Keyhole Route — 14.5 miles round trip, extremely strenuous.
7. Tundra Communities Trail — 1.8 miles round trip, easy, at 12,000 feet elevation.

Camping:
The park has five campgrounds. Moraine Park (244 sites) and Glacier Basin (150 sites) are the largest and require advance reservations from late May through September. Aspenglen (54 sites) is also reservable. Longs Peak (26 sites, tent only) is first-come, first-served. Timber Creek (98 sites, west side) is reservable in summer.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodge. The gateway town of Estes Park on the east side offers numerous hotels, motels, cabins, and vacation rentals. Grand Lake on the west side is a quieter, charming mountain town with lodging options.

Tips for Visitors:
- Acclimatize to altitude before strenuous hikes — the entire park is above 8,000 feet. Drink extra water and take it slow your first day.
- Afternoon thunderstorms at high elevation are extremely dangerous — be at or below treeline by noon.
- Book timed entry permits as soon as they become available (typically on a rolling 1-month basis).
- The Bear Lake corridor shuttle is free and avoids parking headaches — park at the Estes Park Visitor Center and ride in.
- For elk rut, be in Moraine Park or Horseshoe Park at sunrise and sunset in September.
- Temperatures drop dramatically at altitude — bring layers even in summer.
