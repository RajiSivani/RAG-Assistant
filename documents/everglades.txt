Everglades National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Everglades National Park is located at the southern tip of Florida and was established as a national park on December 6, 1947. It covers 2,357 square miles (6,105 sq km) — the largest subtropical wilderness in the United States and the largest national park east of the Mississippi. The park protects the southern portion of the Florida Everglades — a vast, slow-moving "river of grass" (a term coined by author Marjory Stoneman Douglas) that flows from Lake Okeechobee to Florida Bay. The Everglades is an ecosystem found nowhere else on Earth — a mosaic of sawgrass prairies, mangrove forests, pine flatwoods, hardwood hammocks, and marine environments. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, International Biosphere Reserve, and Ramsar Wetland of International Importance. The park is home to a remarkable concentration of endangered and threatened species.

Location and Access:
The park has two main entrances: the Ernest F. Coe Visitor Center near Homestead, Florida (main entrance via FL-9336) and the Shark Valley entrance off US-41 (Tamiami Trail) northwest of Miami. A third area, the Gulf Coast Visitor Center, is located in Everglades City on the park's northwest boundary. Miami International Airport is approximately 40 miles north of the main entrance. The park is often combined with a visit to Biscayne National Park, which is adjacent to the north.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Winter (December to April) is the ideal season — the dry season brings spectacular wildlife viewing as animals concentrate around remaining water sources. Temperatures are comfortable (65-80°F), mosquitoes are reduced (though never absent), and all facilities are open. This is peak visitor season. Summer (May to November) is the wet season — water levels rise, temperatures soar (85-95°F), humidity is extreme, and mosquitoes are ferocious. However, this is when the park is most lush and green, baby alligators and wading birds are active, and crowds are minimal. Hurricanes are possible June through November.

Top Attractions:
1. Anhinga Trail: The most popular trail in the park and one of the best wildlife walks in America — a 0.8-mile paved and boardwalk loop where wildlife appears to have no fear of humans. Anhinga birds dry their wings on railings, alligators bask feet away, herons hunt for fish, and turtles sun on logs. Exceptional year-round, overwhelming in winter.
2. Shark Valley: A 15-mile loop road (bicycle, tram, or on foot) through sawgrass prairie with exceptional alligator sightings — in winter, hundreds of alligators congregate in and around the road. A 65-foot observation tower at the midpoint offers sweeping views across the river of grass.
3. Royal Palm Area: Home to the Anhinga Trail and the adjacent Gumbo Limbo Trail — a 0.4-mile boardwalk through a hardwood hammock with tropical tree species and a canopy thick with air plants.
4. Pa-hay-okee Overlook: A raised boardwalk to a platform offering a classic "river of grass" panorama — a sea of sawgrass extending to the horizon, giving a sense of the immensity of this unique landscape.
5. Nine Mile Pond Canoe Trail: A marked 5.2-mile canoe loop through mangroves and open ponds — an intimate way to explore the backcountry by water with excellent wildlife viewing.
6. Flamingo: At the southern tip of the mainland park, Flamingo offers boat tours into Florida Bay, kayaking through mangrove wilderness, and the opportunity to see American crocodiles, roseate spoonbills, and manatees.
7. Gulf Coast (Everglades City area): The gateway to the Ten Thousand Islands — a vast labyrinth of mangrove islands accessible by canoe or kayak. Sea kayaking here is a world-class experience.

Wildlife:
The Everglades is one of the most biodiverse protected areas in the world. American alligators are ubiquitous — the park has approximately 200,000. American crocodiles (much rarer than alligators) nest at Flamingo. Florida panthers — one of the most endangered mammals in North America — live in the park. The park hosts over 360 bird species including iconic wading birds: great blue herons, great egrets, snowy egrets, tricolored herons, wood storks, roseate spoonbills, and anhingas. Manatees, sea turtles, bottlenose dolphins, and bull sharks inhabit Florida Bay and coastal waters.

Top Hiking and Paddling Trails:
1. Anhinga Trail — 0.8 miles loop, easy, world-class wildlife.
2. Gumbo Limbo Trail — 0.4 miles loop, easy, hammock forest.
3. Pa-hay-okee Overlook — 0.4 miles round trip, easy, panoramic views.
4. Snake Bight Trail — 4 miles round trip, moderate, excellent for flamingos (rare but possible).
5. Eco Pond Loop — 0.5 miles loop, easy, excellent birding near Flamingo.
6. Nine Mile Pond Canoe Trail — 5.2 miles loop, moderate paddling.
7. Wilderness Waterway — 99 miles one way, the ultimate paddling adventure.

Camping:
Long Pine Key Campground (108 sites) is near the main entrance — reservable at recreation.gov in winter, first-come first-served in summer. Flamingo Campground (234 sites) is at the south end of the park — newly renovated with electric hookups available. Backcountry camping (by boat or canoe only) at chickees (raised wooden platforms) and ground sites is available with an advance permit.

Where to Stay:
The newly renovated Flamingo Lodge offers glamping-style eco-tents and lodge rooms with views of Florida Bay — a major improvement from previous basic facilities. Miami and Homestead provide the widest range of hotel options as base camps.

Tips for Visitors:
- ALWAYS bring insect repellent — mosquitoes are legendary in the Everglades year-round.
- The Anhinga Trail is must-see — plan to spend at least an hour there regardless of your itinerary.
- Wildlife is most concentrated in the dry season (Dec-April) along water sources.
- The park is vast — allow multiple days to explore both the main road and the Gulf Coast area.
- Drinking water is available only at certain points — bring extra.
- Alligators are everywhere — maintain respectful distances and never feed them (it is illegal and dangerous).
- Tram tours at Shark Valley are excellent for those who don't want to bike the full 15-mile loop.
