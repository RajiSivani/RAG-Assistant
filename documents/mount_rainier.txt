Mount Rainier National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Mount Rainier National Park is located in Washington State and was established on March 2, 1899, making it the fifth national park established in the United States. It covers 369 square miles (956 sq km) and is dominated by Mount Rainier — an active stratovolcano standing 14,411 feet above sea level, the highest peak in the Cascade Range and the most glaciated peak in the contiguous United States. The mountain holds 26 named glaciers covering approximately 35 square miles and is considered one of the most dangerous volcanoes in the world due to its proximity to the Seattle-Tacoma metropolitan area and the massive lahars (volcanic mudflows) it would produce in an eruption.

Location and Access:
The park is located approximately 90 miles southeast of Seattle, Washington. There are four entrance areas. The Nisqually Entrance on the southwest side is the most popular and the only year-round entrance, leading to Longmire and Paradise. The Stevens Canyon Entrance on the southeast side leads to Ohanapecosh. The White River Entrance on the northeast side leads to Sunrise. The Carbon River Entrance on the northwest side accesses a rainforest area. Seattle-Tacoma International Airport is the nearest major airport.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $30 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $25, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $15, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.
Note: Timed entry reservations are required for vehicles entering the Paradise and Sunrise areas from late May through September. Reserve at recreation.gov.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (mid-July to September) is the primary season — wildflower meadows at Paradise are among the most spectacular in North America, peaking in late July and August. Roads to Paradise and Sunrise are fully open. Temperatures at Paradise (5,400 feet) average 55-65°F with clear mornings often giving way to clouds. Fall (October) brings early snow and autumn color but closing facilities. Winter (November to April) sees heavy snowfall — Paradise averages 54 feet of snow annually and once recorded the deepest snowpack in North America (367 inches). Cross-country skiing and snowshoeing are popular. Summer visits require reservations and parking is extremely limited.

Top Attractions:
1. Paradise: The most visited area of the park at 5,400 feet elevation, offering the most accessible views of the mountain's glaciers and some of the most famous wildflower meadows in the country. The Henry M. Jackson Visitor Center is here along with numerous trail options.
2. Sunrise: The highest point in the park accessible by road at 6,400 feet, on the northeast side of the mountain. Offers closer, dramatic views of the mountain's Emmons Glacier (largest glacier in the lower 48) and excellent wildlife watching for elk and marmots. Less crowded than Paradise.
3. Reflection Lakes: A series of small lakes near Paradise that on calm mornings create perfect mirror reflections of Mount Rainier — one of the most photographed spots in the Pacific Northwest.
4. Skyline Trail Loop: A 5.5-mile loop from Paradise through wildflower meadows to Panorama Point (6,800 feet) with sweeping views of the mountain and surrounding Cascades.
5. Longmire: The park's historic center featuring the original 1920s park buildings, a small museum, a wilderness information center, and the National Park Inn. Lower elevation (2,760 feet) means it's accessible year-round.
6. Grove of the Patriarchs: An island of ancient old-growth forest in the Ohanapecosh River containing massive Douglas firs, western red cedars, and hemlocks — some over 1,000 years old. A flat, paved 1.3-mile trail loops through these giants.
7. Carbon Glacier: The lowest-elevation glacier in the contiguous United States, accessible via a backcountry trail. The Carbon River area contains one of the few inland temperate rainforests in Washington.

Wildlife:
Mount Rainier supports diverse wildlife across its elevation zones. Animals include black bears, mountain lions, elk (excellent viewing at Sunrise meadows), black-tailed deer, mountain goats (visible on high rocky slopes), Olympic marmots (abundant at Sunrise and Paradise), pikas, coyotes, red foxes, and over 260 bird species. The park is excellent for birding including white-tailed ptarmigan, Clark's nutcracker, Steller's jay, and numerous raptors.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Grove of the Patriarchs — 1.3 miles round trip, easy, ancient old-growth trees.
2. Skyline Trail Loop — 5.5 miles round trip, moderate-strenuous, wildflowers and mountain views.
3. Alta Vista Summit — 1.7 miles round trip, moderate, excellent Paradise views.
4. Comet Falls — 3.8 miles round trip, moderate-strenuous, one of the park's tallest waterfalls.
5. Spray Park — 8.4 miles round trip, strenuous, spectacular wildflower meadows off beaten path.
6. Wonderland Trail — 93 miles loop around the entire mountain, extremely strenuous multi-day.
7. Camp Muir — 8.8 miles round trip, extremely strenuous, reaches 10,188 feet at base camp for summit climbers.
8. Sunrise Rim Trail — 5 miles round trip, moderate, excellent views of Emmons Glacier.

Climbing Mount Rainier:
Mount Rainier is the most glaciated peak in the lower 48 and climbing to its summit is a serious mountaineering endeavor. Approximately 10,000 climbers attempt the summit each year with about 50% success rate. All climbers must register with the park and pay a $52 per person climbing fee. The most popular route is the Disappointment Cleaver route via Camp Muir. Glacier travel skills, rope team experience, and the use of crampons and ice axes are required. Most climbers use a guide service.

Camping:
The park has four drive-in campgrounds. Cougar Rock and Ohanapecosh are the largest and most popular — reserve at recreation.gov. White River and Mowich Lake campgrounds are also available. Backcountry camping requires a permit ($6 per person per night). The Wonderland Trail has designated backcountry camps around the mountain.

Where to Stay:
Paradise Inn (historic, 1917) at Paradise and National Park Inn at Longmire are the only in-park lodges — both managed by Aramark. Paradise Inn has a dramatic great room with timber beams. Book 12+ months in advance for summer. Ashford, Packwood, and Enumclaw outside the park offer additional lodging options.

Tips for Visitors:
- Book timed entry reservations for Paradise and Sunrise immediately when they open.
- Weather on Mount Rainier changes extremely rapidly — bring full rain gear and warm layers even in summer.
- The mountain is only visible (cloud-free) about 60 days per year — check the forecast for "Rainier is out."
- Wildflower season at Paradise (late July to mid-August) is absolutely spectacular and worth planning around.
- Start hikes early — parking lots fill by 9am on summer weekends and afternoon clouds often obscure the mountain.
- The Wonderland Trail is a bucket-list backpacking experience requiring 7-14 days and advance planning.
