Virgin Islands National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Virgin Islands National Park is located on the island of St. John in the U.S. Virgin Islands and was established on August 2, 1956. It covers 23 square miles (60 sq km) of land and approximately 5,650 acres of adjacent waters, protecting about 60% of St. John's total land area. The park is famous for its stunning white sand beaches, crystal-clear turquoise waters, vibrant coral reefs, historic sugar plantation ruins, and tropical dry forest. The park was largely funded by a donation from Laurance Rockefeller, who purchased land on St. John to protect it from development. Virgin Islands National Park is one of the most popular national parks in the Caribbean.

Location and Access:
Virgin Islands National Park is located on St. John, U.S. Virgin Islands, approximately 40 miles east of Puerto Rico and adjacent to the British Virgin Islands. There is no commercial airport on St. John. The primary access route is by ferry from St. Thomas — regular passenger ferry service (20-30 minutes) runs between Red Hook or Charlotte Amalie on St. Thomas and Cruz Bay on St. John. Car barges also operate from Red Hook to Cruz Bay. Cyril E. King Airport on St. Thomas (served by multiple U.S. mainland carriers) is the primary arrival point. Many visitors stay on St. Thomas and day trip or overnight trip to St. John.

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for Virgin Islands National Park.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to all national parks.
Note: Maho Bay and Cinnamon Bay campgrounds charge fees for tent sites and camping equipment.

Best Time to Visit:
Winter and Spring (December to May) is the peak and best season. Temperatures are warm but not oppressive (75-85°F), humidity is lower, and the chance of hurricanes is essentially zero. The period from December through February sees the most favorable conditions and highest visitor numbers — book accommodations many months ahead. Summer and Fall (June to November) is the hurricane season and visits require monitoring tropical weather. June and July are typically manageable with just heat and humidity; August through October carries the highest hurricane risk. Despite this, summer offers the clearest water visibility for snorkeling and diving.

Top Attractions:
1. Trunk Bay: One of the most photographed beaches in the Caribbean, with brilliant white sand, turquoise water, and an underwater snorkeling trail with labeled coral formations. A national park fee is charged for day use. The beach can be extremely crowded when cruise ship passengers arrive — visit early morning.
2. Cinnamon Bay: A wide, beautiful bay with a beach, a campground, historic sugar mill ruins, a water sports center, and snorkeling. One of the most complete visitor experiences in the park.
3. Annaberg Sugar Plantation: Remarkably preserved ruins of an 18th-century Danish sugar plantation with mill tower, slave quarters, and windmill ruins. Ranger-led historical tours are offered. The plantation overlooks the British Virgin Islands.
4. Hawksnest Bay: A beautiful, less crowded beach with excellent snorkeling and a picnic area. One of the first bays encountered on the road from Cruz Bay, and often quieter than Trunk Bay.
5. Reef Bay Trail: The park's premier hiking trail — a 5-mile round trip descent through tropical dry forest to a secluded beach passing Taino petroglyphs carved into rocks above a pool. Ranger-led hikes with boat return are offered seasonally (highly recommended — book early).
6. Ram Head Trail: A moderately strenuous 2-mile round trip hike to a dramatic rocky headland at the island's southern tip with panoramic views of St. John's coastline and the British Virgin Islands.

Wildlife:
The marine environment is the park's greatest wildlife treasure. Coral reefs support sea turtles (hawksbill and leatherback), spotted eagle rays, nurse sharks, queen conch, spiny lobster, and hundreds of reef fish species. The terrestrial habitat supports white-tailed deer (introduced), mongoose (introduced), and numerous lizard species. Humpback whales pass through the waters in winter. Over 160 bird species have been recorded including tropical specialties like the mangrove cuckoo, pearly-eyed thrasher, and brown pelican.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Lind Point Trail — 2.1 miles round trip, easy-moderate, from Cruz Bay to Salomon and Honeymoon Beaches.
2. Cinnamon Bay Nature Loop — 0.5 miles round trip, easy, through dry tropical forest to sugar mill ruins.
3. Annaberg Plantation Trail — 0.5 miles round trip, easy, through historic sugar plantation ruins.
4. Ram Head Trail — 2 miles round trip, moderate, to dramatic headland viewpoint.
5. Reef Bay Trail — 5 miles round trip, moderate, through diverse forest to beach and Taino petroglyphs.
6. Johnny Horn Trail — 4.7 miles round trip, strenuous, remote trail with excellent views.

Camping:
Cinnamon Bay Campground is the only in-park campground — one of the most unique campgrounds in the National Park System, located directly on a beautiful Caribbean beach. Options include eco-tents, bare sites, and cottages. Reservations required at recreation.gov. Book months in advance for winter season. The campground reopened in 2022 after hurricane damage repairs.

Where to Stay:
The Caneel Bay Resort (historically operated under a NPS special use permit) has been closed and its future is uncertain — check current status. Cruz Bay and the rest of St. John offer numerous hotels, villas, and vacation rentals. Staying on St. Thomas in Charlotte Amalie or Red Hook and taking the ferry to St. John daily is a cost-effective alternative.

Tips for Visitors:
- Arrive at Trunk Bay before 9am to beat the cruise ship day-trippers — the beach becomes very crowded midday.
- Bring reef-safe sunscreen only — chemical sunscreens harm the coral reefs and are banned in the USVI.
- Rent a snorkel set for your entire stay — the snorkeling throughout the park's bays is consistently excellent.
- The Reef Bay Trail ranger-led hike with boat return is one of the best ranger programs in the entire national park system — book in advance.
- Car rental on St. John is strongly recommended for exploring beyond Cruz Bay — roads are narrow and driving is on the left.
- Stay multiple nights — day trips from St. Thomas don't do justice to the park's beaches and trails.
- Hurricane Ian damaged infrastructure in 2022 — verify current facility status before visiting.
