Grand Teton National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Grand Teton National Park is located in northwestern Wyoming and was established on February 26, 1929. It covers 485 square miles (1,256 sq km) and sits directly south of Yellowstone National Park — many visitors combine both parks in a single trip. The Teton Range rises dramatically from the flat Jackson Hole valley floor with virtually no foothills, making the peaks appear taller than they are. The tallest peak, Grand Teton, stands at 13,775 feet. The Snake River winds through the valley, and a string of glacial lakes at the base of the mountains including Jenny Lake and Jackson Lake create one of the most photogenic landscapes in North America.

Location and Access:
The park is located in northwestern Wyoming, with Jackson, Wyoming (known as Jackson Hole) as the primary gateway town 12 miles south of the park. The main entrance is at Moose, accessed from Highway 89/191. The north end of the park connects directly to Yellowstone via the John D. Rockefeller Jr. Memorial Parkway. Jackson Hole Airport (JAC) is the only commercial airport located within a national park, just south of Jackson. Salt Lake City (280 miles) and Idaho Falls (90 miles) airports are alternatives.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days. The same ticket covers both Grand Teton and Yellowstone if visited within the 7-day period.
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (mid-June to August) is peak season — all facilities open, wildflowers in bloom, and warm valley temperatures (75-85°F) though cool in the mountains. Wildlife is very active. Fall (September to October) is excellent — elk and moose are highly visible, aspen trees turn golden, and crowds thin significantly after Labor Day. Fall is considered by many photographers to be the most beautiful season. Spring (May to early June) brings pronghorn fawns, bear activity, and dramatic snowmelt but some roads and facilities remain closed. Winter (November to March) brings cross-country skiing, snowshoeing, and the magical sight of bison and elk in snow-covered meadows.

Top Attractions:
1. Oxbow Bend: A slow meander in the Snake River offering arguably the most iconic reflection view in the entire national park system — the Grand Teton peaks mirrored in the calm water. Dawn and dusk are prime times for photography and moose sightings.
2. Jenny Lake: The jewel of Grand Teton, a crystal-clear glacial lake at the foot of the mountains. A scenic boat shuttle ($18 round trip) crosses the lake, shortening the hike to Hidden Falls and Inspiration Point. The 7.7-mile Jenny Lake Loop Trail is among the best day hikes in the park.
3. Snake River Overlook: The exact spot where Ansel Adams took his famous photograph of the Tetons in 1942. Still one of the most photographed landscapes in America.
4. Schwabacher Landing: A dirt road leads to a series of beaver ponds on the Snake River with perfect Teton reflections — excellent for photography and moose watching at dawn.
5. Mormon Row Historic District: A row of photogenic historic homestead barns from the late 1800s with the Teton Range as backdrop — one of the most photographed spots in the park.
6. Signal Mountain Summit Road: A 5-mile paved road to the summit of Signal Mountain offering a 360-degree panoramic view of Jackson Hole valley and the Teton Range.
7. Laurance S. Rockefeller Preserve: A quieter section of the park with trails through forests, wildflower meadows, and to Phelps Lake — one of the best birding areas in the park.

Wildlife:
Grand Teton is outstanding for wildlife viewing. The park and surrounding National Elk Refuge host approximately 7,500 elk in winter — one of the largest elk herds in North America. Other animals include grizzly bears, black bears, gray wolves, moose (excellent near Oxbow Bend and Willow Flats), pronghorn, bison, mule deer, coyotes, river otters, bald eagles, osprey, and trumpeter swans. The park is considered one of the best places in America to see moose.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Jenny Lake Loop — 7.7 miles round trip, moderate, classic lake and mountain scenery.
2. Hidden Falls and Inspiration Point — 5.8 miles (or 2 miles with boat shortcut), moderate.
3. Cascade Canyon — 9.9 miles round trip, moderate, stunning glacially-carved canyon.
4. Death Canyon — 8 miles round trip, moderate-strenuous.
5. Taggart and Bradley Lakes Loop — 6 miles round trip, moderate, two glacial lakes.
6. Grand Teton Summit (via Upper Exum Ridge) — requires technical climbing skills and a guide.
7. Paintbrush-Cascade Canyon Loop — 18.5 miles, strenuous multi-day backpack, among the best loops in the Rockies.

Camping:
The park has five campgrounds. Gros Ventre (350 sites, largest) and Jenny Lake (49 sites, tent only — most scenic) are most popular. Signal Mountain, Colter Bay, and Headwaters (Flagg Ranch) round out the options. Reservations accepted at recreation.gov — Jenny Lake fills immediately when reservations open.

Where to Stay:
Jenny Lake Lodge is the most exclusive in-park option — a classic dude ranch experience. Jackson Lake Lodge offers stunning views of the Tetons from its lobby windows. Colter Bay Village and Signal Mountain Lodge provide mid-range in-park choices. Jackson, Wyoming is a charming, upscale mountain town 12 miles south with extensive lodging and dining options.

Tips for Visitors:
- The single best time for Teton photography is the 30 minutes before sunrise at Oxbow Bend or Schwabacher Landing.
- The combined Grand Teton-Yellowstone entrance pass is excellent value — buy it at either park.
- Wildlife is most active at dawn and dusk — plan your schedule accordingly.
- The Teton Range is one of the youngest mountain ranges in the Rockies — geologically fascinating.
- Jackson, Wyoming is an excellent base — it has outstanding restaurants, shopping, and the famous Town Square elk antler arches.
- Afternoon storms are common in summer — start hikes early.
