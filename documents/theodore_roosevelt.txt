Theodore Roosevelt National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Theodore Roosevelt National Park is located in the Badlands of western North Dakota and was established on November 10, 1978. It covers 110 square miles (285 sq km) divided into three separate units: the South Unit near Medora, the North Unit near Watford City, and the Elkhorn Ranch Unit (the site of Theodore Roosevelt's original ranches, with no facilities). The park is named for President Theodore Roosevelt, who came to the North Dakota Badlands as a young man in 1883 and developed his love for the American West and his conservation philosophy here. The park protects dramatically eroded badlands landscape, mixed-grass prairies, and exceptional wildlife including free-roaming bison, wild horses, and elk.

Location and Access:
The South Unit entrance is in Medora, North Dakota, just off I-94 at Exit 24, approximately 135 miles west of Bismarck. The North Unit entrance is approximately 70 miles north of Medora via U.S. Highway 85, near Watford City. The nearest major airports are Bismarck Municipal Airport (135 miles east of the South Unit) and Williston Basin International Airport (70 miles north of the North Unit). Medora is the primary gateway community.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $30 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $25, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $15, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Late Spring to Fall (May to October) is the primary visitor season. Summer (June to August) is peak season with maximum wildlife activity, warm temperatures (70-95°F), and all facilities operating. Fall (September to October) is arguably the best time — the grass turns golden, the crowds thin, and wildlife is very active. Bison rut occurs in July-August, making for dramatic wildlife viewing. Spring (April to May) brings wildflowers and newborn bison calves. Winter closes most facilities and road conditions can be dangerous — the park transforms into a remote snowy wilderness with dramatically fewer visitors.

Top Attractions:
1. Free-Roaming Bison Herds: The park's approximately 700 bison are the most compelling wildlife attraction. Unlike most parks where wildlife sightings require luck, bison are almost guaranteed — large herds frequently block the scenic loop drives in both the South and North Units. During bison rut (July-August), bulls can be seen fighting and displaying.
2. South Unit Scenic Loop Drive: A 36-mile paved loop drive through the South Unit badlands, passing numerous overlooks, prairie dog towns, and frequent wildlife sightings. Highlights include the Painted Canyon Overlook (actually outside the park entrance) and the Wind Canyon Trail.
3. North Unit Scenic Drive: A 14-mile dead-end drive into the rugged North Unit, ending at the Oxbow Overlook with sweeping views of the Little Missouri River gorge and badlands. The North Unit is significantly less visited and offers more solitude.
4. Wild Horses: A herd of approximately 60-100 feral horses roams the South Unit — a rare and thrilling wildlife spectacle. Horses are most commonly seen in the early morning in the open prairie areas of the loop drive.
5. Prairie Dog Towns: Large, active prairie dog colonies ("towns") are found throughout the South Unit and are easily observed from pullouts along the scenic drive. Prairie dogs are gregarious and provide entertaining wildlife viewing — they attract black-footed ferrets (extremely rare and critically endangered, occasionally reintroduced here) and numerous raptors.
6. Petrified Forest: A remote backcountry area of the South Unit accessible only by hiking (10-mile round trip, strenuous) or horseback, featuring petrified wood scattered across an eroded badlands landscape.

Wildlife:
Beyond bison and wild horses, the park supports remarkable wildlife. Elk herds are commonly seen, especially in the North Unit. Pronghorn graze the open prairie. White-tailed and mule deer are abundant. Bighorn sheep were reintroduced and can sometimes be seen. Coyotes, badgers, and prairie dogs are commonly observed. Golden eagles and ferruginous hawks hunt over the prairie. The Little Missouri River and associated bottomlands attract over 185 recorded bird species.

Top Hiking Trails:
South Unit:
1. Ridgeline Trail — 0.6 miles round trip, easy, panoramic badlands views near visitor center.
2. Coal Vein Trail — 0.8 miles round trip, easy, interpretive trail on a former burning coal seam.
3. Wind Canyon Trail — 0.4 miles round trip, easy, scenic overlook above the Little Missouri River.
4. Painted Canyon Nature Trail — 1 mile round trip, easy, from the Painted Canyon Visitor Center.
5. Petrified Forest Loop — 10.4 miles round trip, strenuous, to remote petrified wood area.

North Unit:
1. Squaw Creek Trail — 1.5 miles round trip, easy, through badlands landscape.
2. Buckhorn Trail — 11.4 miles round trip, strenuous, through the North Unit wilderness.
3. Caprock Coulee Trail — 4.4 miles round trip, moderate, through sculpted badlands.

Camping:
Cottonwood Campground in the South Unit (76 sites) and Juniper Campground in the North Unit (50 sites) are the primary campgrounds. Cottonwood accepts reservations through recreation.gov. Juniper is first-come, first-served. Both campgrounds offer excellent wildlife viewing right from camp — bison frequently walk through the campgrounds.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodge. Medora, North Dakota is the charming western gateway town to the South Unit. Medora has several hotels and the famous Roughriders Hotel. Medora Musical, a popular outdoor musical show, runs nightly in summer. Watford City serves the North Unit.

Tips for Visitors:
- Never approach bison — they are extremely dangerous despite appearing docile. Maintain at least 75 feet distance. Bison can run 35 mph and have charged and injured park visitors.
- Drive the South Unit loop in the early morning for the best wildlife and light — bison, horses, and deer are most active at dawn and dusk.
- The Painted Canyon Overlook (just east of Medora at Exit 32 off I-94) offers a spectacular first view of the badlands — stop even if just passing through.
- The North Unit is significantly less visited — a worthwhile side trip for solitude and different badlands scenery.
- Check for ticks after any hike — the grasslands harbor ticks throughout the season.
- The park is very family-friendly — wildlife sightings are frequent and reliable, making it rewarding for children.
- Medora Musical is a beloved local institution — a fun evening entertainment option for those staying in Medora.
