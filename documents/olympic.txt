Olympic National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Olympic National Park is located on the Olympic Peninsula in the state of Washington and was established as a national park on June 29, 1938. It covers 1,442 square miles (3,735 sq km) making it one of the larger national parks in the contiguous United States. The park is extraordinary for its ecological diversity — it contains three distinct ecosystems within a relatively small area: glacier-capped mountains, temperate rainforest, and 73 miles of wild Pacific coastline. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and International Biosphere Reserve. The Hoh Rain Forest receives up to 167 inches of rain per year, making it one of the wettest places in the continental United States.

Location and Access:
The park is located on the Olympic Peninsula in northwestern Washington State, approximately 90 miles west of Seattle. The peninsula is bordered by Puget Sound to the east, the Pacific Ocean to the west, and the Strait of Juan de Fuca to the north. Access is primarily from Highway 101, which loops around most of the peninsula. The gateway town of Port Angeles on the north side offers a ferry connection to Victoria, Canada. Seattle-Tacoma International Airport is the nearest major airport. There is no road through the center of the park — each area is accessed via a spur road off the Highway 101 loop.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $30 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $25, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $15, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (July to September) is the best time for the mountains and coast — the window of dry weather is relatively short at this latitude. Hurricane Ridge is snow-free from July to September. Fall (October to November) brings spectacular color to the rainforest but increasingly heavy rainfall on the coast and in the Hoh. Winter (December to April) is the wettest season — the rainforest is lush and green, the coast is wild and dramatic, but mountain access is limited. Spring (May to June) sees wildflowers and waterfalls at peak flow, though rain is still frequent.

Top Attractions:
1. Hoh Rain Forest: One of only a handful of temperate rainforests in the world. Massive Sitka spruce, western red cedar, and big-leaf maple trees are draped in thick curtains of club moss, creating an ancient, otherworldly landscape. The Hall of Mosses Trail (0.8 miles, easy) is one of the most magical walks in any national park.
2. Hurricane Ridge: Located 17 miles south of Port Angeles at 5,242 feet elevation, Hurricane Ridge offers stunning views of glacier-capped Olympic peaks and the Strait of Juan de Fuca. The subalpine meadows are filled with wildflowers in summer and Olympic marmots and black-tailed deer are frequently seen roadside.
3. Rialto Beach: A dramatic stretch of wild Pacific coastline with sea stacks, tidepools, and driftwood-strewn shores. The walk to Hole-in-the-Wall — a large natural arch carved by waves — is a classic Olympic experience.
4. Ruby Beach: Perhaps the most photographed beach in the Pacific Northwest — sea stacks rise dramatically from the surf, sunset colors are extraordinary, and tide pools teem with life.
5. Sol Duc Hot Springs: Natural hot springs in the heart of the rainforest, with three developed soaking pools. The surrounding area offers excellent hiking including Sol Duc Falls — one of the most dramatic waterfalls in the park.
6. Lake Crescent: A deep, glacially-carved lake of stunning turquoise clarity, set between steep forested mountains. Kayak and paddleboat rentals available at Lake Crescent Lodge.
7. Mount Olympus: At 7,980 feet, the highest peak in the park. Heavily glaciated — the Blue Glacier on its flanks is one of the most studied glaciers in the United States. Reaching the summit requires technical climbing skills.

Wildlife:
Olympic's isolation on the peninsula has led to several unique endemic species found nowhere else. The Olympic marmot, Olympic torrent salamander, and Olympic mudminnow exist only on the Olympic Peninsula. Other animals include Roosevelt elk (the largest herd of Roosevelt elk in the world — approximately 5,000), black bears, mountain lions, black-tailed deer, river otters, bald eagles, and over 300 bird species. The coast hosts harbor seals, sea lions, and occasionally gray whales and orcas.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Hall of Mosses — 0.8 miles round trip, easy, iconic rainforest loop.
2. Hoh River Trail — up to 17.4 miles one way (backpacking), easy grade, old-growth forest.
3. Hurricane Hill — 3.2 miles round trip, moderate, panoramic mountain views.
4. Sol Duc Falls — 1.6 miles round trip, easy, stunning multi-channeled waterfall.
5. Rialto Beach to Hole-in-the-Wall — 3 miles round trip, easy, dramatic coastal walk.
6. High Divide-Bailey Range Loop — 18.5 miles, strenuous, considered among the best backpacking loops in Washington.
7. Seven Lakes Basin — 20.5 miles, strenuous multi-day, alpine lakes and meadows.

Camping:
Olympic has 13 developed campgrounds spread around the park's perimeter. Hoh, Kalaloch (oceanfront), and Sol Duc are most popular and reservable at recreation.gov. Several smaller campgrounds are first-come, first-served. Backcountry camping requires a permit ($8 per person per night).

Where to Stay:
Lake Crescent Lodge (historic, 1916), Sol Duc Hot Springs Resort, and Kalaloch Lodge (oceanfront) are in-park lodging options. Port Angeles on the north side is the main gateway town with numerous hotels. Forks (famous as the setting for the Twilight novels) is the gateway for the west side rainforest and coast.

Tips for Visitors:
- The park has four distinct areas (mountains, rainforest, coast, lakes) — plan which zones to prioritize as driving between them takes time.
- Always check tide tables before hiking on coastal beaches — some sections are impassable at high tide.
- Bring full rain gear regardless of season — weather changes rapidly.
- The Hoh Rain Forest is worth visiting even in rain — it's most atmospheric in misty conditions.
- Hurricane Ridge Road can close with little warning due to weather — check conditions before driving up.
- Cell service is extremely limited throughout the park and non-existent in most rainforest and coast areas.
