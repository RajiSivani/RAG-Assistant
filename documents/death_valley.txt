Death Valley National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Death Valley National Park is located on the border of California and Nevada and was established as a national park on October 31, 1994 (previously a national monument since 1933). It is the largest national park in the contiguous United States, covering 5,270 square miles (13,650 sq km). Death Valley holds the record for the highest air temperature ever recorded on Earth — 134°F (56.7°C), measured at Furnace Creek on July 10, 1913. Badwater Basin, at 282 feet below sea level, is the lowest point in North America. Despite its seemingly inhospitable reputation, Death Valley is a place of astonishing geological diversity and stark, otherworldly beauty — featuring sand dunes, salt flats, volcanic craters, colorful badlands, canyons, and snow-capped peaks rising over 11,000 feet above the valley floor.

Location and Access:
Death Valley is located in the Mojave Desert of eastern California near the Nevada border. The main visitor hub is Furnace Creek, accessible via CA-190 from the west or east. Las Vegas, Nevada is 140 miles east — the most common starting point for visitors. Los Angeles is 290 miles west. Multiple entrance roads approach from all directions. Furnace Creek Airport offers small aircraft access. The nearest commercial airports are Las Vegas (140 miles) and Fresno (250 miles).

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Fall (November to February) is the ideal season — temperatures are comfortable (65-75°F in the valley) and all areas are accessible. This is the peak visitor season. Spring (March to April) can bring spectacular desert wildflower blooms in good rainfall years — superbloom events draw massive crowds. Temperatures are rising but still manageable. Summer (May to September) is life-threateningly dangerous at low elevations — temperatures routinely exceed 120°F and the ground surface can reach 200°F. Only the high elevation areas (Telescope Peak at 11,049 feet) are accessible without serious heat risk. Do NOT hike in the valley in summer.

Top Attractions:
1. Badwater Basin: The lowest point in North America at 282 feet below sea level — a vast salt flat extending for miles. An ethereal landscape of polygonal salt formations. Walk out onto the flats for a surreal experience. The elevation sign on the cliff above places the sea level position for perspective.
2. Mesquite Flat Sand Dunes: The most accessible dunes in the park, rising up to 100 feet. Magnificent at sunrise and sunset when shadows emphasize their curves. No marked trails — just wander. Star trails photos here are extraordinary.
3. Artist's Drive and Artist's Palette: A 9-mile one-way scenic loop through multi-colored hills painted with oxides of various metals — green (mica), red (iron oxide), yellow (sulfur), and purple (manganese). Midday light bleaches the colors; late afternoon is best.
4. Zabriskie Point: One of the most iconic viewpoints in Death Valley — a panorama of deeply eroded golden badlands, best at sunrise when the formations glow. Immortalized in film and photography worldwide.
5. Dante's View: A viewpoint at 5,475 feet overlooking the entire valley — Badwater Basin is visible directly below and the Sierra Nevada (including Mount Whitney, the highest peak in the lower 48) is visible to the west. A stunning perspective of the region's dramatic elevation change.
6. Ubehebe Crater: A half-mile-wide volcanic crater in the north of the park, formed by a massive steam explosion approximately 2,000-7,000 years ago. A short trail to the rim offers views 600 feet down into the crater.
7. Racetrack Playa: A remote, mysteriously flat dry lake bed famous for its "sailing stones" — rocks that mysteriously move across the flat surface, leaving tracks. The mystery was solved in 2014 (thin ice sheets move the rocks). The journey to reach it (27 miles of rough dirt road) is itself an adventure.

Wildlife:
Death Valley supports over 1,000 plant species (51 found only here), 400 bird species, 51 mammal species, 30 reptile species, and 3 endemic pupfish species that survive in isolated springs despite the extreme heat. The Amargosa pupfish and Devils Hole pupfish live in water holes that have been continuously inhabited for 10,000-20,000 years. Coyotes, kit foxes, bighorn sheep, wild burros, and sidewinder rattlesnakes are commonly observed.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Natural Bridge Canyon — 1.8 miles round trip, easy-moderate, natural arch.
2. Golden Canyon to Gower Gulch — 4.3 miles loop, moderate, badlands and red cathedral.
3. Mosaic Canyon — 4 miles round trip, moderate, polished marble slot canyon.
4. Marble Canyon — 8 miles round trip, moderate, narrow and beautiful canyon.
5. Telescope Peak — 14 miles round trip, strenuous, highest point in park (11,049 ft).
6. Fall Canyon — 8 miles round trip, moderate, dramatic canyon narrows.

Camping:
Furnace Creek Campground (136 sites) is the primary campground — reservable October through April. Mesquite Spring (30 sites) in the north is year-round and first-come, first-served. Texas Spring (92 sites) is open October through April, first-come first-served. Numerous free primitive backcountry camping areas exist throughout the park.

Where to Stay:
The Inn at Death Valley (formerly Furnace Creek Inn) is a historic luxury resort — one of the great desert resort experiences in America. The Ranch at Death Valley offers more affordable rooms and a campground. Stovepipe Wells Village provides basic motel-style accommodations in the north valley.

Tips for Visitors:
- NEVER underestimate the heat — people die of heat stroke in Death Valley every year.
- Carry a minimum of 4 liters of water per person and more for any hiking.
- Check tire pressure before entering — heat causes tires to expand and blow out.
- Cell service is essentially nonexistent outside Furnace Creek.
- The best stargazing in the lower 48 states is here — certified by the International Dark-Sky Association.
- Superbloom events (rare, after heavy rainfall) are worth a special trip — check the park website in February.
- Gas is available at Furnace Creek and Stovepipe Wells but is expensive — fill up in a gateway town.
