Black Canyon of the Gunnison National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Black Canyon of the Gunnison National Park is located in western Colorado and was established as a national park on October 21, 1999 (previously a national monument since 1933). It covers 48 square miles (124 sq km). The park protects the most dramatic and awe-inspiring section of the Gunnison River canyon — a narrow, nearly vertical gorge carved through some of the Earth's oldest rocks (1.7 billion years old). The canyon is so deep and narrow that some sections receive as little as 33 minutes of sunlight per day — which is why early explorers named it the "Black" canyon. The deepest section plunges 2,722 feet from rim to river, while the canyon walls are as narrow as 40 feet at the river level. It is one of the least-visited yet most spectacular national parks in the country.

Location and Access:
Black Canyon is located near Montrose, Colorado, approximately 15 miles east of the city. The South Rim is the most accessible and developed side, reached via US-50 east of Montrose (Highway 347 turnoff). The North Rim is more remote, accessed via unpaved road from Crawford — and is closed in winter. The nearest major airport is Grand Junction Regional Airport (65 miles northwest). The park can be combined with a visit to Curecanti National Recreation Area, which borders the park to the east.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $20 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $15, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $10, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Late Spring (May to June) and Fall (September to October) are ideal — mild temperatures (60-80°F on the rim), all facilities open, and excellent visibility for photography. Summer (July to August) is the peak season with warm temperatures and all facilities operating, though afternoon thunderstorms are common. The South Rim is open year-round, while winter brings snow and cold but stunning scenery with very few visitors — some overlooks require snowshoes. The North Rim is typically closed November through April.

Top Attractions:
1. South Rim Road: A 7-mile paved road along the South Rim with 12 overlooks offering increasingly dramatic views into the canyon. Gunnison Point near the visitor center, Chasm View, and Painted Wall View are highlights.
2. Painted Wall: The tallest cliff face in Colorado at 2,250 feet — taller than any cliff in the Grand Canyon. Streaked with dramatic pink and white pegmatite intrusions that create the appearance of brush strokes on the dark rock. Visible from multiple overlooks.
3. Chasm View Nature Trail: A 0.3-mile loop trail that leads to the canyon's narrowest point where the walls are only 1,100 feet apart at the top and 40 feet apart at the river.
4. Warner Point Nature Trail: A 1.5-mile round trip trail to the most expansive panorama on the South Rim, with views stretching to the San Juan Mountains.
5. North Rim: Far less visited than the South Rim, the North Rim feels genuinely wild. The road ends at a spectacular overlook — the canyon here is narrower and even more dramatic.
6. Inner Canyon Routes: The park offers several extremely strenuous unmarked routes — really scrambles down near-vertical walls — to reach the Gunnison River at the canyon bottom. These are not trails but designated routes (Gunnison, S.O.B., Warner, etc.) requiring a free inner canyon permit.

Wildlife:
The canyon and surrounding terrain support mule deer, elk, black bears, mountain lions, bobcats, peregrine falcons, golden eagles, and ravens. The Gunnison River at the bottom supports a blue-ribbon trout fishery. Rock squirrels and Steller's jays are commonly seen at the rim overlooks. Marmots are frequently spotted among the talus slopes.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Rim Rock Nature Trail — 1 mile loop, easy, near the visitor center.
2. Cedar Point Nature Trail — 0.7 miles round trip, easy, two dramatic overlooks.
3. Chasm View Nature Trail — 0.3 miles loop, easy, views at the narrowest point.
4. Warner Point Nature Trail — 1.5 miles round trip, moderate, best panoramic views.
5. North Vista Trail (North Rim) — up to 7 miles round trip to Exclamation Point, moderate-strenuous.
6. Gunnison Route (Inner Canyon) — 2 miles one way, extremely strenuous scramble, free permit required.

Camping:
South Rim Campground (88 sites) is open year-round — sites C and D loops can be reserved at recreation.gov, while the A loop is first-come, first-served. North Rim Campground (13 sites) is primitive, first-come, first-served, and open mid-May through November. Inner canyon camping is permitted at the river with a backcountry permit.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodging. The city of Montrose (15 miles from the South Rim) offers the widest range of hotels and services. The small town of Crawford provides basic services for North Rim visitors. Gunnison and Crested Butte to the east offer charming mountain town accommodations.

Tips for Visitors:
- The canyon walls are sheer and unstable — stay behind all fences and barriers at overlooks.
- Inner canyon routes are not trails — they are technical scrambles. Only attempt them with proper experience, gear, and a free permit from the visitor center.
- Bring layers — rim temperatures can be 20-30°F cooler than surrounding lowlands, and wind at the rim is strong.
- Photography is spectacular at sunrise on the South Rim (when the Painted Wall is lit) and late afternoon on the North Rim.
- The park is small enough to see the South Rim's highlights in a single day, but deserves more time.
- Fishing the Gunnison River at the bottom requires a Colorado fishing license and a gold-medal section permit.
