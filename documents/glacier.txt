Glacier National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Glacier National Park is located in northwestern Montana along the Canadian border and was established on May 11, 1910. It covers 1,013 square miles (2,623 sq km) and together with Canada's Waterton Lakes National Park forms Waterton-Glacier International Peace Park, designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The park contains over 700 miles of trails, 762 lakes, and more than 200 waterfalls. It is named for the glaciers carved by ancient ice — though climate change has dramatically reduced the glaciers from 150 in 1850 to approximately 25 active glaciers today. Scientists project the remaining glaciers may disappear by 2030.

Location and Access:
The park is located in northwestern Montana, near the towns of Whitefish and Kalispell. The west entrance near Apgar is the most popular. The east side is accessed through the towns of Browning and St. Mary. Going-to-the-Sun Road connects the west and east sides through the heart of the park. The nearest major airport is Glacier Park International Airport (FCA) in Kalispell, approximately 30 miles from the west entrance. Amtrak's Empire Builder train stops at East Glacier Park and West Glacier stations.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.
Note: A vehicle reservation (timed entry permit) is required to drive Going-to-the-Sun Road between Apgar and St. Mary from late May through mid-September. Reservations open in March at recreation.gov and sell out quickly.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (July to August) is the peak season and the only time Going-to-the-Sun Road is fully open. Temperatures are mild (65-75°F in valleys) but snow can fall at any time at high elevations. Wildflowers peak in July. Fall (September) is quieter, with beautiful foliage, fewer crowds, and wildlife activity including bear hyperphagia (intense pre-hibernation feeding). Spring (May to June) has snowmelt waterfalls and wildlife with newborns, but Going-to-the-Sun Road progressively opens throughout June. Winter (October to April) sees most services close, but snowshoeing and cross-country skiing are available and the solitude is extraordinary.

Top Attractions:
1. Going-to-the-Sun Road: Considered one of the most scenic drives in North America, this 50-mile road crosses the Continental Divide at Logan Pass (6,646 feet). Carved into sheer mountain faces with views of glaciers, waterfalls, and alpine meadows. The road is a National Historic Landmark.
2. Logan Pass: The highest point on Going-to-the-Sun Road, this alpine area offers exceptional wildlife watching (mountain goats and bighorn sheep are commonly seen roadside), wildflower meadows, and the start of the Hidden Lake and Highline Trails.
3. Hidden Lake Overlook Trail: A 2.7-mile round trip moderate trail from Logan Pass through prime mountain goat habitat to a stunning overlook above Hidden Lake. One of the most rewarding short hikes in the park.
4. Highline Trail: Beginning at Logan Pass, this iconic 11.8-mile trail traverses the Garden Wall ridge at high elevation with constant panoramic views. The first section hugs a sheer cliff face with a cable to hold — not for those afraid of heights.
5. Many Glacier Area: The most remote and arguably most spectacular area of the park, on the east side. Surrounded by five glaciers and multiple lakes, it offers exceptional hiking, wildlife watching, and the historic Many Glacier Hotel.
6. Bowman Lake: A pristine backcountry lake accessible via a rough dirt road on the north side. Crystal clear water, dramatic peaks, and far fewer crowds than the Going-to-the-Sun corridor.
7. St. Mary Lake: The second-largest lake in the park, framed by dramatic peaks and one of the most photographed spots in Glacier.

Wildlife:
Glacier is one of the few remaining intact mountain ecosystems in the world and supports exceptional wildlife. Animals include grizzly bears (approximately 300), black bears, gray wolves, wolverines, mountain lions, lynx, mountain goats (commonly seen at Logan Pass), bighorn sheep, moose, elk, deer, coyotes, and pikas. Over 260 bird species have been recorded. The park is one of the best places in the lower 48 to see grizzly bears in the wild.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Trail of the Cedars — 0.9 miles, easy, accessible loop through old-growth cedar and hemlock.
2. Hidden Lake Overlook — 2.7 miles round trip, moderate, mountain goats and alpine scenery.
3. Avalanche Lake — 4.5 miles round trip, easy-moderate, stunning cirque lake.
4. Iceberg Lake — 9.7 miles round trip, moderate, lake with floating icebergs in summer.
5. Highline Trail — 11.8 miles one way, moderate-strenuous, classic Glacier experience.
6. Grinnell Glacier — 10.6 miles round trip, strenuous, one of the few accessible glaciers.
7. Siyeh Pass — 10.3 miles, strenuous, crosses the Continental Divide.

Camping:
The park has 13 campgrounds. Apgar, Fish Creek, St. Mary, and Many Glacier are the most popular and can be reserved (recreation.gov). Two Medicine, Cut Bank, and others are first-come, first-served. Backcountry camping requires a permit ($7 per person per night) and most sites must be reserved in advance starting in mid-March.

Where to Stay:
The historic Many Glacier Hotel (1915) and Lake McDonald Lodge (1913) are iconic in-park options managed by Pursuit — book 13 months in advance when reservations open. Village Inn at Apgar and Rising Sun Motor Inn offer simpler in-park lodging. Gateway towns include Whitefish (charming ski town), Kalispell, and East Glacier Park.

Tips for Visitors:
- Reserve Going-to-the-Sun Road vehicle permits in March — they sell out within minutes.
- The free park shuttle system covers Going-to-the-Sun Road — an excellent alternative to driving.
- Always carry bear spray (available to rent in gateway towns) and know how to use it.
- Logan Pass parking fills by 8am in summer — use the shuttle.
- Many Glacier is worth the extra drive — it's far less crowded than the west side.
- Visit the glaciers soon — they are disappearing rapidly due to climate change.
- Late June-early July offers the best combination of open road, peak wildflowers, and waterfalls.
