Sequoia National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Sequoia National Park is located in the southern Sierra Nevada of central California and was established on September 25, 1890, making it the second-oldest national park in the United States after Yellowstone. It covers approximately 631 square miles (1,635 sq km) and is administered jointly with the adjacent Kings Canyon National Park by the National Park Service — the two parks share a continuous boundary and are managed as a single unit, though they remain legally distinct parks. Sequoia was established primarily to protect the giant sequoias (Sequoiadendron giganteum) — the largest trees on Earth by volume — which grow in groves along the western slopes of the Sierra Nevada. The park's centerpiece is the Giant Forest, home to the General Sherman Tree, the largest living tree on Earth by volume at 52,508 cubic feet. Beyond its famous trees, Sequoia protects a vast wilderness of granite peaks, deep canyons, subalpine meadows, and the highest summit in the contiguous United States — Mount Whitney (14,505 feet) — which lies just outside the park boundary on the adjacent John Muir Wilderness but is most commonly approached through the park's eastern edges.

Location and Access:
Sequoia National Park is located in Tulare County in central California, approximately 65 miles east of Visalia. The primary entrance is the Ash Mountain Entrance on Highway 198, approximately 7 miles east of Three Rivers. From the Ash Mountain Entrance, the Generals Highway climbs steeply 16 miles to the Giant Forest — the park's main attraction — passing through dense forest and switchbacks with dramatic views. The Generals Highway continues north through the park, connecting to Kings Canyon National Park at Grant Grove. The nearest major airports are Fresno Yosemite International Airport (approximately 85 miles northwest) and the smaller Visalia Municipal Airport. Visalia (approximately 50 miles west) and Three Rivers (7 miles west of the entrance) are the primary gateway communities. No commercial transportation serves the park — a personal vehicle is required, though a free shuttle system operates within the Giant Forest and Lodgepole areas in summer.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days (covers both Sequoia and Kings Canyon National Parks).
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.
Note: The entry fee covers both Sequoia and Kings Canyon National Parks together.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (June to August) is peak season — all roads and facilities are open, the Giant Forest is snow-free, and long days allow for extensive exploration. Temperatures at Giant Forest (elevation 6,400 feet) are pleasant at 65-80°F, while lower elevations near the Ash Mountain Entrance can exceed 100°F in July and August. Late Spring (late May to June) is exceptional — waterfalls are at peak flow, wildflowers bloom across the meadows, and crowds are lighter than July and August. Fall (September to October) is arguably the best time to visit — golden light filters through the sequoias, temperatures are ideal, and crowds diminish significantly. Winter transforms the Giant Forest into a snow-draped wonderland — the giant sequoias laden with snow are breathtaking, and snowshoeing and cross-country skiing are popular. The Generals Highway can close temporarily during heavy snowstorms, but the Giant Forest is generally accessible year-round with chains when required.

Top Attractions:
1. General Sherman Tree: The largest living tree on Earth by volume — 274.9 feet tall, 102.6 feet in circumference at its base, and estimated to be 2,200 years old. Located in the Giant Forest, the tree is reached via a 0.8-mile round trip paved trail from the upper parking area or a longer descent from the main Generals Highway. The sheer scale of the tree is difficult to comprehend until you are standing at its base. The surrounding Giant Forest contains five of the ten largest trees in the world.
2. Giant Forest: The world's finest grove of giant sequoias, containing over 8,000 mature giant sequoias across a 3-square-mile area on the western slope of the Sierra Nevada. The Giant Forest is threaded by over 40 miles of trails and is the spiritual heart of the park. The Giant Forest Museum at the center of the grove provides excellent interpretation of sequoia ecology and natural history.
3. Moro Rock: An iconic granite dome rising abruptly from the Giant Forest, reached by a steep 0.5-mile staircase trail (350 steps) to a summit at 6,725 feet. The 360-degree panorama from the top takes in the Great Western Divide, the Kaweah River canyon, the San Joaquin Valley (on clear days), and the surrounding Sierra Nevada peaks. One of the most dramatic viewpoints in the Sierra Nevada and an iconic Sequoia experience.
4. Tunnel Log: A giant sequoia that fell across the Crescent Meadow Road in 1937 and was subsequently tunneled through to allow vehicle passage — one of the park's most beloved quirky attractions. Vehicles (under 8 feet tall) drive directly through the fallen trunk. A bypass trail accommodates larger vehicles and pedestrians who prefer to walk around.
5. Crescent Meadow: A beautiful subalpine meadow at the end of the Crescent Meadow Road, ringed by giant sequoias and crossed by a network of gentle trails. John Muir described Crescent Meadow as the "Gem of the Sierra." The meadow and surrounding trails offer some of the most serene and uncrowded sequoia experiences in the park, particularly in the early morning when deer and bears are frequently seen.
6. Crystal Cave: A stunning marble cavern located in the park's backcountry, accessible via a 0.5-mile trail from the Crystal Cave Road. Guided tours (45 minutes to several hours) explore formations of stalactites, stalagmites, flowstone, and rare cave coral. Tickets must be purchased in advance at the Foothills or Lodgepole visitor centers — they cannot be purchased at the cave itself. Open seasonally from mid-May through November.
7. High Sierra Trail: One of the premier long-distance trails in the Sierra Nevada, beginning at Crescent Meadow and traversing 72 miles of spectacular backcountry to Whitney Portal near Mount Whitney. Even the first few miles from Crescent Meadow offer extraordinary views of the Great Western Divide across the Kaweah River canyon — accessible as a day hike for those not continuing to Whitney.

Wildlife:
Sequoia supports abundant Sierra Nevada wildlife. Black bears are common and highly active — the park has one of the highest black bear densities in California. Bear canisters are required for all overnight backcountry travel, and bear-resistant boxes are provided at all campgrounds and trailheads. Mule deer are extremely common and frequently seen in meadows and near the Giant Forest. Mountain lions are present throughout the park but rarely encountered. Coyotes and bobcats inhabit the lower elevation foothills. Marmots, pikas, and golden-mantled ground squirrels are common at higher elevations. The park's rivers support rainbow trout and the threatened golden trout in higher elevation streams. Steller's jays, Clark's nutcrackers, acorn woodpeckers, and white-headed woodpeckers are characteristic birds of the giant sequoia forest. Peregrine falcons nest on granite cliff faces. Over 200 bird species have been recorded in the combined Sequoia-Kings Canyon area.

Top Hiking Trails:
Giant Forest Area:
1. General Sherman Tree Trail — 0.8 miles round trip, easy, paved trail to the world's largest tree by volume.
2. Congress Trail — 2 miles round trip, easy, paved loop through the heart of the Giant Forest past the General Sherman Tree, the House and Senate groups of sequoias, and the President Tree.
3. Big Trees Trail — 1.3 miles round trip, easy, flat paved loop around Round Meadow through giant sequoias — fully accessible and ideal for families.
4. Moro Rock Trail — 0.5 miles round trip, moderate (350 steps), to panoramic summit views of the Great Western Divide and Kaweah Canyon.
5. Crescent Meadow Loop — 1.8 miles round trip, easy, through the "Gem of the Sierra" meadow and sequoia forest past Tharp's Log (a historic cabin in a hollowed sequoia).
6. High Sierra Trail (first segment) — variable length, easy-moderate, from Crescent Meadow along the canyon rim with dramatic views of the Great Western Divide.

Foothills Area:
7. Marble Falls Trail — 7 miles round trip, moderate-strenuous, through chaparral and oak woodland to a cascading waterfall in the Marble Fork of the Kaweah River — best in spring.
8. Middle Fork Trail — variable length, moderate, along the Middle Fork Kaweah River through foothill woodland — excellent for spring wildflowers and early-season access when Giant Forest roads may still be closed.

Backcountry:
The park's wilderness encompasses a vast expanse of the southern Sierra Nevada from the canyon bottoms to peaks above 13,000 feet. The High Sierra Trail to Mount Whitney is the park's signature long-distance route. The Lakes Trail (12.4 miles round trip from Wolverton) reaches the spectacular Pear Lake and Heather Lake basin in the high country. Wilderness permits are required for all overnight backcountry travel and are obtained through a quota reservation system via recreation.gov.

Camping:
Lodgepole Campground (214 sites) is the largest and most central campground, located near the Lodgepole Visitor Center and accessible year-round. Reservations are strongly recommended through recreation.gov for summer. Dorst Creek Campground (218 sites) is located in a quieter area of the park near a sequoia grove and is open seasonally. Potwisha Campground (42 sites) is located in the warm Foothills area near the Ash Mountain Entrance and is open year-round — a good base for spring visits before the Giant Forest road fully clears. Buckeye Flat Campground (28 sites, tents only) is another Foothills option. Backcountry camping is permitted throughout the wilderness with a permit. Bear boxes are provided at all campgrounds and are mandatory for food storage.

Where to Stay:
Wuksachi Lodge, operated by Delaware North within the Giant Forest area, is the only in-park lodging — a modern lodge at 7,200 feet elevation offering hotel-style rooms with easy access to the Giant Forest trails. Book 6-12 months in advance for summer and holiday periods. Three Rivers, just outside the Ash Mountain Entrance, offers a range of motels, vacation rentals, and B&Bs — a popular and convenient base for park visitors. Visalia, approximately 50 miles west, offers the full range of hotels and services. Lodging in Three Rivers books up quickly for summer weekends — reserve early.

Tips for Visitors:
- Use the free Giant Forest shuttle in summer — parking at the Giant Forest Museum and General Sherman trailhead fills completely by mid-morning on summer weekends. The shuttle connects Wuksachi Lodge, Lodgepole, the Giant Forest Museum, Moro Rock, and Crescent Meadow, making a car unnecessary once you're in the area.
- Bear canisters are required for backcountry overnight travel and strongly recommended for all hikers carrying food — bears in Sequoia are persistent and experienced. Never leave food, trash, or scented items in your vehicle; bears regularly break into cars.
- Crystal Cave tickets sell out weeks in advance during summer — purchase tickets online before your trip, as they cannot be bought at the cave.
- The drive up the Generals Highway from the Ash Mountain Entrance is steep, winding, and slow — allow at least 45 minutes to reach the Giant Forest from the entrance and do not attempt it with oversized vehicles (RVs and trailers over 22 feet are prohibited).
- The Giant Forest sequoias are most magical in the early morning — soft light filters through the canopy, wildlife is active, and the crowds have not yet arrived. Plan to be on trail by 7am.
- Winter visits to the Giant Forest offer an extraordinary and crowd-free experience — the giant sequoias laden with snow are among the most spectacular sights in the national park system. Carry chains and check road conditions before driving up.
- Air quality can be significantly impaired by wildfire smoke in July through September — check current air quality index before visiting and consider avoiding strenuous activity on high smoke days.
- The park shares an entry fee and management with Kings Canyon — combine both parks in a single trip if possible. The Generals Highway connecting them is one of the most scenic drives in California.
- Altitude affects visitors arriving from sea level — the Giant Forest sits at 6,400 feet and high country trails exceed 10,000 feet. Allow time to acclimatize, stay hydrated, and watch for symptoms of altitude sickness.
- Cell service is nonexistent through most of the park — download offline maps before your visit and leave a trip itinerary with someone outside the park for any backcountry travel.
