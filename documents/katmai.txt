Katmai National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Katmai National Park and Preserve is located on the Alaska Peninsula in southwestern Alaska and was established as a national park on December 2, 1980 (originally a national monument in 1918 following the 1912 eruption of Novarupta volcano). It covers 6,395 square miles (16,564 sq km), making it one of the largest national parks in the United States. Katmai is world-famous for its brown bear viewing — particularly at Brooks Falls where bears congregate to catch sockeye salmon leaping upstream. The park also protects the Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes, a volcanic landscape left by the 1912 Novarupta eruption — one of the largest volcanic eruptions of the 20th century.

Location and Access:
Katmai is located on the Alaska Peninsula, approximately 290 miles southwest of Anchorage. There is no road access — the park is accessible only by air or boat. The gateway community is King Salmon, Alaska, served by daily flights from Anchorage. From King Salmon, visitors take a float plane or bus (in summer) to Brooks Camp, the main visitor hub within the park. Float plane operators provide direct service from Anchorage to Brooks Camp as well. The park is remote and requires advance planning.

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for Katmai National Park.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to all national parks.
Note: Brooks Camp has a limited number of visitor permits during peak bear viewing season (July and September). Reservations for both lodging and the day-use permit system are required and extremely competitive — apply through recreation.gov up to 6 months in advance.

Best Time to Visit:
July is the prime bear viewing month — sockeye salmon begin their run and bears congregate at Brooks Falls in large numbers. The famous "Fat Bear Week" competition (documenting bears fattening up for hibernation) occurs in late September/early October, when bears gather at Brooks Falls and the Brooks River to feed on late-season salmon. June sees bears feeding on grass and early salmon. The park is generally accessible from June through mid-October. Weather is cool and often rainy — temperatures range from 40-65°F in summer.

Top Attractions:
1. Brooks Falls Bear Viewing: The most iconic wildlife viewing experience in the national park system. A viewing platform at Brooks Falls allows visitors to watch brown bears catch sockeye salmon as they leap up the falls. In peak July season, 30-40 bears may be present simultaneously. The live Brooks Falls webcam (accessed online) has millions of viewers annually.
2. Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes: A remarkable volcanic landscape created by the 1912 Novarupta eruption, which deposited up to 700 feet of volcanic ash and pumice over 40 square miles. The valley is accessible by a daily bus from Brooks Camp and a 23-mile round trip hike. The barren, alien landscape is one of the most unusual geological sites in any national park.
3. Brooks River: The entire 1.5-mile river connecting Brooks Lake to Naknek Lake is prime bear habitat, with multiple viewing platforms. Bears fish, forage, and nurse cubs along the river throughout the summer season.
4. Sport Fishing: Katmai is renowned for world-class sport fishing. The Brooks River, Naknek River, and numerous wilderness lakes and streams offer exceptional fishing for sockeye, king, silver, and pink salmon, as well as rainbow trout and arctic grayling.
5. Kayaking and Canoeing: The park's extensive lake system — including Naknek Lake, Brooks Lake, and the Savonoski Loop — offers extraordinary wilderness paddling through remote bear country. Multi-day expeditions are possible.
6. Novarupta Volcano: The vent responsible for the 1912 eruption, accessible by hiking from the Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes bus drop-off. The dome that plugged the vent is still visible.

Wildlife:
Katmai is home to approximately 2,200 brown bears — one of the highest densities in the world. Watching bears catch salmon is an unmatched wildlife experience. Other mammals include wolves, wolverines, moose, caribou, red foxes, river otters, and sea otters along the coast. Marine mammals including beluga whales, harbor seals, and Steller sea lions inhabit Shelikof Strait on the park's eastern coast. The park has over 230 bird species.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Brooks Camp Beach Trail — 1.5 miles, easy, along Naknek Lake shore with bear viewing opportunities.
2. Brooks Falls Trail — 1.5 miles round trip, easy, to the falls viewing platform.
3. Dumpling Mountain Trail — 4 miles round trip, moderate, to panoramic views over Brooks Lake and the surrounding wilderness.
4. Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes Hike — 23 miles round trip from the bus drop-off, strenuous, through volcanic landscape.
Note: Most of the park is roadless wilderness requiring expert backcountry skills and bear awareness.

Camping:
Brooks Camp Campground (60 sites) requires advance reservations and is extremely competitive — apply through recreation.gov. Bear-proof food storage is mandatory. Dispersed backcountry camping is permitted throughout the park with a free permit. All food must be stored in certified bear-proof containers.

Where to Stay:
Brooks Lodge is the only in-park accommodation — a comfortable wilderness lodge offering cabins, meals, and bear viewing packages. It books up over a year in advance for peak July season. King Salmon has a few basic lodges and hotels for those transiting. Remote wilderness lodges accessible only by float plane are scattered throughout the park.

Tips for Visitors:
- Reserve Brooks Lodge or Brooks Camp reservations 12+ months in advance for peak July bear viewing.
- Take the mandatory bear safety orientation upon arriving at Brooks Camp — it is required for all visitors.
- Always maintain the required 50-yard distance from bears (100 yards from sows with cubs).
- Rain gear is essential — the Alaska Peninsula receives heavy precipitation throughout the summer.
- Bring quality binoculars for bear and wildlife viewing from the platforms.
- The live Brooks Falls webcam allows virtual viewing at explore.org — plan your trip around peak salmon run timing.
- Consider hiring a local guide service for backcountry fishing and kayaking expeditions.
