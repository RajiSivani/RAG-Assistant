White Sands National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
White Sands National Park is located in southern New Mexico and was designated a national park on December 20, 2019, after 87 years as a national monument. It covers 226 square miles (585 sq km) and protects the world's largest gypsum dunefield — 275 square miles of dazzling white gypsum sand dunes that make up the heart of the Tularosa Basin. Unlike typical quartz sand dunes, gypsum sand does not absorb heat from the sun, making the dunes cool and comfortable even in summer. The pure white dunes create an otherworldly, luminous landscape that is unlike anywhere else on Earth. The park is surrounded by White Sands Missile Range, a U.S. military installation, which periodically closes the highway through the park for missile testing.

Location and Access:
White Sands is located 15 miles southwest of Alamogordo, New Mexico, via U.S. Highway 70. It is approximately 84 miles north of El Paso, Texas and 52 miles east of Las Cruces, New Mexico. El Paso International Airport (84 miles south) is the nearest major airport. Alamogordo (15 miles northeast) is the primary gateway community. Note: U.S. Highway 70 through the park is occasionally closed for missile testing at White Sands Missile Range — closures are typically 2-3 hours and announced on the park website.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $25 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $15, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Spring (March to May) and Fall (September to November) offer the most comfortable temperatures for hiking (65-85°F). Summer (June to September) daytime temperatures reach 95-105°F, but the dunes stay cool underfoot, and the afternoon monsoon season (July-September) brings dramatic storm clouds and occasional rainbows over the white dunes. The park is particularly spectacular on summer evenings. Winter (December to February) temperatures are cold (30-65°F) with occasional snow — the sight of white gypsum dunes under a light dusting of real snow is extraordinary. Full moon nights at any time of year are magical — the dunes glow in the moonlight.

Top Attractions:
1. The Dunefield: Simply walking, rolling, running, and playing in the spectacular white gypsum dunes is the primary experience. Unlike rocky or heavily vegetated parks, White Sands is interactive — visitors can hike freely, sled, build dune sculptures, and explore the moonscape. The dunes reach up to 60 feet in height.
2. Dunes Drive: An 8-mile paved and unpaved scenic road extending into the heart of the dunefield, with five trailheads, picnic areas, and interpretive stops. The road ends at an accessible picnic area surrounded by large dunes.
3. Sunset and Sunrise: White Sands is world-famous for its sunsets and sunrises. As the light changes, the dunes shift from brilliant white to warm pink, orange, and purple. The park hosts popular Full Moon nights during warmer months (free special events with extended hours).
4. Sand Sledding: Saucer-shaped plastic sleds can be purchased at the gift shop or brought from outside — sledding down the steep dune faces is one of the most joyful activities in any national park. The gypsum sand provides faster sliding than regular sand.
5. Lake Lucero: A dry lakebed at the southwest corner of the park (source of the gypsum that forms the dunes) — accessible only on ranger-led tours offered monthly. The selenite crystals (gypsum in crystal form) on the lakebed are spectacular.
6. Alkali Flats Trail: A backcountry trail crossing the open interdune flats and larger dunefield — one of the most unique hiking experiences in the park system, following cairns across an expanse of white.

Wildlife:
Despite appearing barren, White Sands supports remarkable life. Several animal species have evolved white coloration as camouflage against the gypsum — the bleached earless lizard, Apache pocket mouse, and others represent rapid evolutionary adaptation. Coyotes, kit foxes, badgers, black-tailed jackrabbits, and pronghorn inhabit the park. Roadrunners are commonly seen year-round. Diamondback rattlesnakes are present. Burrowing owls and other raptors hunt the dunes. The park has recorded over 100 bird species.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Interdune Boardwalk — 0.4 miles round trip, easy, accessible, interpretive trail through interdunal plants.
2. Desert Nature Trail — 1 mile round trip, easy, through desert scrub and low dunes.
3. Playa Trail — 2 miles round trip, easy-moderate, to a seasonal playa (dry lake).
4. Dune Life Nature Trail — 1 mile round trip, moderate, through medium-sized dunes.
5. Alkali Flats Trail — 5 miles loop, strenuous, across the open dunefield (follow cairns — easy to get disoriented in flat light).
6. Backcountry Camping Trail — 2 miles one way, requires backcountry permit, to designated camping sites in the dunes.

Camping:
There is one backcountry campsite area (10 sites) in the dunefield, accessible by a 2-mile hike from the end of Dunes Drive. Permits are required and available at the visitor center the day of or the day before the stay. There are no RV hookups or developed frontcountry campgrounds in the park. The nearest campgrounds are in Alamogordo and Oliver Lee Memorial State Park.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodge. Alamogordo (15 miles east) is the primary gateway with hotels, motels, and restaurants. Las Cruces (52 miles west) and El Paso (84 miles south) offer a much wider range of accommodations. Many visitors combine White Sands with Carlsbad Caverns National Park (90 miles east), making a southern New Mexico road trip.

Tips for Visitors:
- Check the park website or call before visiting for U.S. Highway 70 missile test closures — the road closes 2-4 times per week for testing, typically for 1-3 hours.
- Visit at sunset for the most spectacular photography — the dunes turn warm gold and pink in the evening light.
- Full Moon nights are extraordinary — the park extends hours and the illuminated dunes under moonlight are magical.
- Buy or bring a plastic sled — sand sledding is enormously fun and one of the park's most memorable activities.
- The Alkali Flats Trail should not be hiked in poor visibility or stormy conditions — the flat terrain and absence of landmarks makes it easy to become disoriented.
- Bring sun protection even in winter — the white sand reflects sunlight intensely, creating significant UV exposure from all angles.
- The park pairs perfectly with a stop at the New Mexico Museum of Space History in Alamogordo.
