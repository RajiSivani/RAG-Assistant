National Park of American Samoa - Complete Guide

Overview:
The National Park of American Samoa is the only United States national park south of the equator, located in the South Pacific Ocean territory of American Samoa. It was established on October 31, 1988, making it one of the newer national parks. The park covers 13,500 acres spread across three islands: Tutuila, Ofu, and Ta'ū. The park protects tropical rainforest, coral reefs, and the traditional Samoan culture (fa'a Samoa) that has persisted for over 3,000 years. It is one of the least visited national parks due to its remote location, receiving fewer than 10,000 visitors per year.

Location and Access:
The park is located in American Samoa, a U.S. territory in the South Pacific, approximately 2,600 miles southwest of Hawaii. The main hub is Pago Pago on the island of Tutuila. Access is via Pago Pago International Airport, which receives flights from Honolulu, Hawaii (approximately 5.5 hours). Inter-island ferry or small aircraft service connects Tutuila to the outer islands of Ofu and Ta'ū. There are no cruise ship connections. Visitors should plan well in advance as transportation options are limited.

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for the National Park of American Samoa.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to all national parks but has limited applicability here since there is no fee.
Note: The park has a unique homestay program — visitors can stay with local Samoan families (village chiefs arrange accommodations). Contact the park in advance to arrange this.

Best Time to Visit:
The park is open year-round. The dry season (May to October) is generally the best time to visit, with lower humidity, less rainfall, and calmer ocean conditions ideal for snorkeling and diving. The wet season (November to April) brings heavy tropical rains and occasional cyclones — Samoa is in a cyclone-prone region. Temperatures remain warm year-round, averaging 80-85°F, with high humidity throughout.

Top Attractions:
1. Ofu Beach and Coral Reef: Considered one of the most pristine coral reefs in the entire National Park System. The coral reef off Ofu Beach is spectacularly healthy, with exceptional visibility and abundant marine life including sea turtles, reef fish, and corals. Snorkeling here is world-class.
2. Mount Alava Trail: The most popular hiking trail on Tutuila, climbing 1,610 feet to the summit of Mount Alava with sweeping views over Pago Pago Harbor and the surrounding rainforest. A historic aerial tramway (currently non-operational) once connected the summit to the harbor.
3. Tropical Rainforest: The park protects ancient tropical rainforest containing over 900 plant species, including paleotropical forest types found nowhere else in the U.S. park system. Flying foxes (large fruit bats) are a signature wildlife species.
4. Ta'ū Island: The most remote and wild section of the park, featuring dramatic sea cliffs up to 3,000 feet — among the tallest sea cliffs in the world — and pristine forest. The island is home to the largest population of the endemic Pacific boa.
5. Pola Island Trail: A short trail on the island of Ofu leading through coastal forest to scenic overlooks above the Ofu-Olosega strait.
6. Samoan Cultural Experience: The park works closely with local villages to preserve fa'a Samoa (the Samoan way). Visitors can engage with traditional fishing practices, weaving, and dance through village-arranged cultural programs.

Wildlife:
The park supports extraordinary marine biodiversity — over 950 fish species and 250 coral species inhabit its reefs. On land, three species of flying foxes (fruit bats) are ecologically critical pollinators and seed dispersers. The park is also home to the Pacific boa, sheath-tailed bats, numerous seabirds including white terns and red-footed boobies, and several endemic land birds. Sea turtles (green and hawksbill) nest on park beaches.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Mount Alava Trail — 7 miles round trip, strenuous, summit views over Pago Pago Harbor.
2. Pola Island Trail — 1.5 miles round trip, moderate, coastal forest and views.
3. World War II Heritage Trail — 2.8 miles, moderate, passes WWII-era gun emplacements and military history sites on Tutuila.
4. Lower Sauma Ridge Trail — 3.4 miles round trip, moderate, through rainforest with flying fox viewing opportunities.

Camping:
There are no developed campgrounds in the park. Accommodation is primarily through the park's Homestay Program, where visitors stay with local Samoan families in traditional villages. This is a unique cultural experience and must be arranged in advance through the park or village chiefs. Basic guesthouses and small hotels are available in Pago Pago.

Where to Stay:
The Homestay Program is the recommended and most culturally immersive option — contact the park visitor center to arrange stays in villages near the park units. Pago Pago offers a small number of hotels including the Tradewinds Hotel. Accommodations are limited and should be booked well in advance.

Tips for Visitors:
- Apply for the homestay program months in advance — it fills up and requires coordination with village authorities.
- Bring reef-safe sunscreen only — chemical sunscreens are harmful to the pristine coral reefs and may be restricted.
- Respect local Samoan customs — dress modestly, especially in villages, and observe Sunday as a day of rest and worship.
- Bring all necessary medications and supplies — medical facilities on the outer islands are very limited.
- The marine environment is the highlight — invest in quality snorkel gear or rent it on Tutuila.
- Travel insurance including medical evacuation coverage is strongly recommended given the remote location.
- Currency is the U.S. dollar; credit cards are accepted in Pago Pago but cash is needed in villages.
