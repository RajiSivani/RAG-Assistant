Gates of the Arctic National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Gates of the Arctic National Park and Preserve is located entirely above the Arctic Circle in northern Alaska and was established as a national park on December 2, 1980. It covers 13,238 square miles (34,287 sq km) — the second largest national park in the United States. There are no roads, maintained trails, visitor facilities, or campgrounds within the park. It is the northernmost national park in the country and one of the most remote and least-visited protected areas on Earth, receiving only about 10,000 visitors per year. The park takes its name from two peaks — Frigid Crags and Boreal Mountain — described as the "gates" of the Arctic by explorer Robert Marshall in the 1930s. The park encompasses the central Brooks Range, vast tundra river valleys, and glacially-carved lakes of extraordinary beauty.

Location and Access:
Gates of the Arctic is located in northern Alaska, centered on the Brooks Range. The nearest town with scheduled air service is Bettles, Alaska (accessed by small plane from Fairbanks), which serves as the main gateway. Coldfoot, on the Dalton Highway, is another gateway. The Dalton Highway (AK-11) passes just east of the park and provides limited vehicle access to the eastern fringe. Most visitors fly in by bush plane from Fairbanks (250 miles south) or Bettles to remote lake or gravel bar landing strips within the park. The park has no visitor center inside its boundaries — the Bettles Visitor Contact Station and the Arctic Interagency Visitor Center in Coldfoot serve as information points.

Entry Fees:
The park is free to enter. However, bush plane transportation costs are significant — $500-$1,200+ per person round trip depending on destination and operator.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (late June to early August) offers 24-hour daylight, the best weather, accessible river travel, and active wildlife. Temperatures range from 40-70°F but can drop to freezing unexpectedly. July is peak month but still sees very few visitors. Mosquitoes can be intense in early summer. August brings fewer mosquitoes and early fall color. Fall (late August to September) offers spectacular tundra color, caribou migrations, and fewer mosquitoes — but weather deteriorates rapidly. Winter brings extraordinary aurora borealis viewing and extreme cold (-40°F possible).

Top Attractions:
1. Brooks Range Peaks: A vast, jagged mountain range stretching 700 miles across Alaska — the only major mountain range in the world that runs east-west above the Arctic Circle. The Arrigetch Peaks are the most dramatic, featuring granite spires rising from verdant valleys.
2. Arctic Rivers: The park's rivers — Alatna, John, Noatak, North Fork of the Koyukuk — offer world-class wilderness river paddling and rafting. Multi-day float trips are the primary way most serious visitors experience the park.
3. Northern Lights (Aurora Borealis): From late August through April, the aurora borealis is visible on clear nights — one of the most vivid displays in North America due to the extreme northern latitude.
4. Caribou Migration: The Western Arctic caribou herd (approximately 190,000 animals) and the Central Arctic herd migrate through the park in spring and fall — a wildlife spectacle rivaling Africa.
5. Grizzly Bear and Wolf Viewing: The park supports healthy populations of grizzly bears and wolves. Viewing them in their completely undisturbed, vast natural habitat is a rare and profound experience.
6. Lake Matcharak and Takahula Lake: Beautiful glacially-carved lakes serving as float plane landing areas — bases for exploration and fishing.

Wildlife:
Gates of the Arctic contains the full complement of Arctic and subarctic wildlife in a completely undisturbed state. Species include grizzly bears, black bears, wolves, wolverines, lynx, moose, caribou (in massive herds during migrations), Dall sheep, musk oxen (east side), beavers, river otters, Arctic foxes, and ground squirrels. Over 170 bird species are recorded including golden eagles, gyrfalcons, rough-legged hawks, Arctic terns, and ptarmigan. The rivers support native Dolly Varden, Arctic grayling, and lake trout.

Top Activities (No Maintained Trails Exist):
1. Bush Plane Access — Fly in to remote lake or gravel bar with a float or bush plane.
2. River Paddling — Multi-day float trips on the Alatna, Noatak, John, or Koyukuk Rivers.
3. Backpacking — Completely cross-country navigation on open tundra and mountain terrain.
4. Mountaineering — Technical and non-technical routes on Brooks Range peaks.
5. Fishing — Exceptional Arctic grayling, Dolly Varden, and lake trout in pristine waters.
6. Wildlife Watching — Caribou, bears, wolves, and Dall sheep in completely wild conditions.

Camping:
There are no designated campgrounds — camping is entirely primitive and cross-country. Visitors camp wherever conditions allow. A Leave No Trace ethic is essential. Bear-resistant food canisters are strongly recommended. Most visitors camp near their float plane drop-off or along river routes.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodging. Bettles Lodge is the most convenient base for park access — offering guided trips, bush plane services, and lodging. Coldfoot Camp on the Dalton Highway provides lodging and serves eastern park access. Fairbanks offers the widest range of accommodations as a staging point.

Tips for Visitors:
- This is a true wilderness expedition — treat it as such. Self-sufficiency is absolutely required.
- Work with experienced bush plane operators and guides, especially on first visits.
- Bear spray is essential — carry it at all times and know how to use it.
- Weather can change extremely rapidly — always have extra food and shelter for unexpected delays.
- Mosquitoes in June and July can be overwhelming — bring head nets and maximum-strength repellent.
- File a detailed trip plan with a responsible contact and the Alaska State Troopers before entering.
- This is an experience for experienced, physically fit wilderness travelers — it is not a casual destination.
