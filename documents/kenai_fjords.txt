Kenai Fjords National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Kenai Fjords National Park is located on the Kenai Peninsula of south-central Alaska and was established on December 2, 1980. It covers 1,047 square miles (2,711 sq km) and is named for the numerous fjords carved by glaciers along its coastline. The park is dominated by the Harding Icefield — one of the largest icefields in the United States — which feeds over 40 glaciers, including the accessible Exit Glacier. The park's rugged coastline, tidewater glaciers, and extraordinarily rich marine ecosystem make it one of Alaska's premier destinations.

Location and Access:
Kenai Fjords is located adjacent to the city of Seward, Alaska, approximately 130 miles south of Anchorage via the Seward Highway (a National Scenic Byway). Seward is the gateway community and primary access point. The Seward Highway from Anchorage is one of Alaska's most scenic drives. Alaska Railroad provides passenger train service between Anchorage and Seward from May through September. The nearest major airport is Ted Stevens Anchorage International Airport (130 miles north).

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for Kenai Fjords National Park.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to all national parks.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (late May to early September) is the only practical visitor season. The Seward Highway and Exit Glacier Road are open. Tour boats operate in Resurrection Bay and to the fjords and tidewater glaciers. Wildlife is most active and abundant. Peak visitor season is July-August with long daylight hours (up to 19 hours). Temperatures are cool (50-65°F) and rain is frequent — the park receives over 60 inches of precipitation annually. Winter closes most facilities and roads, though Exit Glacier is accessible by ski or snowshoe in winter.

Top Attractions:
1. Exit Glacier: The only road-accessible part of the park, located just north of Seward. A network of trails leads to various viewpoints of this advancing and retreating glacier, including the spectacular Upper Trail hike onto the Harding Icefield. Interpretive signs mark the glacier's historical retreat over the past 200 years.
2. Harding Icefield Trail: The most demanding and rewarding day hike in the park — a strenuous 8.2-mile round trip climb gaining 3,000 feet to the edge of the Harding Icefield, one of the largest non-polar icefields in the world. On clear days, views of the vast white expanse are unforgettable.
3. Boat Tours to Tidewater Glaciers: The most popular way to experience the fjords — full-day and half-day boat tours from Seward travel to Aialik Bay and Northwestern Fjord to view active tidewater glaciers calving into the sea. Tours also observe extraordinary marine wildlife including humpback whales, orcas, Steller sea lions, Dall's porpoises, sea otters, and massive seabird colonies.
4. Resurrection Bay: The bay fronting Seward offers excellent marine wildlife viewing even without a full-day fjord tour. Harbor seals, sea otters, Dall's porpoises, and bald eagles are commonly seen.
5. Holgate Glacier and Aialik Glacier: The most visited tidewater glaciers, regularly visited by tour boats. Both glaciers actively calve icebergs and provide spectacular close-up viewing.
6. Seabird Colonies: The park's coastline and offshore rocks host enormous seabird colonies. Tufted puffins, horned puffins, common murres, thick-billed murres, black-legged kittiwakes, and pelagic cormorants nest in the hundreds of thousands. The Chiswell Islands are particularly notable.

Wildlife:
Kenai Fjords has extraordinary marine biodiversity. Humpback whales, orcas, minke whales, Dall's porpoises, harbor porpoises, Steller sea lions, and sea otters are regularly seen on boat tours. Harbor seals haul out on ice floes near tidewater glaciers. Brown bears, black bears, moose, mountain goats, and wolves inhabit the terrestrial zones. The park has over 190 bird species and the seabird colonies are among the most impressive in Alaska.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Exit Glacier Nature Trail — 0.8 miles round trip, easy, views of Exit Glacier terminus.
2. Glacier View Loop — 1.8 miles round trip, moderate, multiple glacier viewpoints and historical retreat markers.
3. Edge of the Glacier Trail — 3 miles round trip, moderate-strenuous, to ice viewing area.
4. Harding Icefield Trail — 8.2 miles round trip, extremely strenuous, 3,000-foot gain to icefield.
Note: The vast majority of the park is accessible only by boat or float plane — backcountry hiking requires wilderness skills and bear awareness.

Camping:
Exit Glacier Campground (12 sites, walk-in tent camping only) near the Exit Glacier trailhead is free and first-come, first-served. Backcountry camping is permitted throughout the park with no permit required. Several public-use cabins are available on Resurrection Bay and in the fjords — reservable through recreation.gov. Sea kayakers often camp on beaches in the fjords.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodge. Seward offers a wide range of accommodations including hotels, B&Bs, vacation rentals, and the Seward Hostel. Book summer accommodations well in advance as Seward is very popular. The Alaska Railroad offers dome car day trips from Anchorage with connections to boat tours.

Tips for Visitors:
- Book boat tours well in advance for July and August — they sell out. Full-day Northwestern Fjord tours offer the best wildlife and glacier viewing.
- The Harding Icefield Trail requires solid fitness and proper preparation — bring layers, rain gear, and bear spray.
- Weather changes rapidly — bring full rain gear even on sunny mornings.
- Sea kayaking in the fjords is extraordinary for those with experience — multi-day trips are possible with water taxi support.
- Check tide tables before beach camping in the fjords — tidal range can be significant.
- Combine the park with the scenic Seward Highway drive from Anchorage for a spectacular Alaska road trip.
- Exit Glacier is accessible year-round (road typically open) and makes a worthwhile winter snowshoe destination.
