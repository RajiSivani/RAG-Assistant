New River Gorge National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
New River Gorge National Park and Preserve is located in southern West Virginia and became America's newest national park on December 27, 2020, when it was redesignated from a national river. It covers 117 square miles (303 sq km) along 53 miles of the New River, which — despite its name — is one of the oldest rivers in North America, estimated to be 3-65 million years old. The park features one of the deepest and longest river gorges in the Appalachians, a rich coal mining heritage, and a world-class rock climbing destination. The New River Gorge Bridge, completed in 1977, was the longest single-arch steel bridge in the world for decades and remains an iconic American engineering achievement.

Location and Access:
New River Gorge is located in Fayetteville, Beckley, and Oak Hill, West Virginia, approximately 60 miles south of Charleston, West Virginia and 240 miles southwest of Washington, D.C. The park is accessible from U.S. Highway 19 (crossing the bridge) and U.S. Highway 60. The Canyon Rim Visitor Center near Fayetteville is the main visitor hub. The nearest major airports are Yeager Airport in Charleston, WV (60 miles) and Roanoke-Blacksburg Regional Airport in Virginia (100 miles). Amtrak's Cardinal line stops in Prince (near the park) three times per week.

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for New River Gorge National Park.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to all national parks.

Best Time to Visit:
Spring (April to early June) and Fall (September to November) are the best seasons. Fall foliage in October transforms the gorge into a spectacular display of color — the gorge is one of the best fall foliage destinations in the eastern United States. Spring brings wildflowers and excellent hiking temperatures. Summer (June to August) is popular for whitewater rafting but can be hot and humid. Winter brings quiet solitude and bare trees that open views of the rock faces — climbing continues year-round for the hardy. The famous Bridge Day festival, held on the third Saturday of October, draws 100,000+ visitors for BASE jumping off the bridge and rappelling.

Top Attractions:
1. New River Gorge Bridge: The iconic single-arch bridge spanning 3,030 feet across the gorge at a height of 876 feet. The best viewpoint is from the Canyon Rim Visitor Center's overlook. Bridge Day (third Saturday of October) is a legendary festival when the bridge is open for pedestrians, BASE jumpers leap from the deck, and the gorge fills with celebration.
2. Whitewater Rafting: The New River and Gauley River (in the adjacent preserve) offer some of the best whitewater rafting in the eastern United States. The Upper New River has Class I-III rapids suitable for families and beginners. The Lower New has Class IV-V rapids for experienced paddlers. October brings extreme water releases on the Gauley — the famous "Gauley Season" — with Class IV-V+ rapids that attract expert paddlers from around the world.
3. Rock Climbing: New River Gorge is one of the premier rock climbing destinations in the eastern United States, with over 1,400 established climbing routes on Nuttall Sandstone cliffs. The area attracts climbers year-round. Popular areas include Endless Wall, Beauty Mountain, and Grandview Rim.
4. Canyon Rim Boardwalk: A paved, accessible 0.5-mile boardwalk along the rim of the gorge from the Canyon Rim Visitor Center, offering spectacular views of the bridge and gorge.
5. Grandview Overlook: A stunning viewpoint on the north rim offering a horseshoe bend view of the New River 1,400 feet below, widely considered the most spectacular view in the park.
6. Historic Thurmond: A remarkably preserved ghost town and former railroad hub within the park, once one of the busiest coal and railroad towns in West Virginia. The depot is restored and staffed by rangers in summer.

Wildlife:
The gorge's diverse habitats support rich biodiversity. White-tailed deer, black bears, wild turkeys, river otters, and bobcats inhabit the park. Peregrine falcons nest on the gorge cliffs. The New River supports smallmouth bass and muskellunge fishing. Over 175 bird species have been recorded. The park is one of the best places in the eastern U.S. to observe spring wildflower displays, including trillium, bloodroot, and Dutchman's breeches on the gorge slopes.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Canyon Rim Boardwalk — 0.5 miles, easy, accessible, bridge views.
2. Long Point Trail — 3.2 miles round trip, moderate, to a spectacular cliff-edge view of the bridge.
3. Endless Wall Trail — 5.4 miles round trip, moderate, along the gorge rim with rock climbing access.
4. Grandview Rim Trail — 5 miles round trip, moderate, to horseshoe bend overlooks.
5. Grandview Sandbar Trail — 1.5 miles round trip, easy-moderate, descends to the river.
6. Grandview Main Overlook Nature Trail — 0.5 miles round trip, easy, to Grandview Overlook.
7. Thurmond-Minden Trail — 3.5 miles one way, moderate, connects two historic communities.

Camping:
Grandview Campground (32 sites), Burnwood Campground (29 sites), and Army Camp (30 sites) offer developed camping. Several primitive campgrounds are available. All campgrounds are first-come, first-served (no reservations). The campgrounds are free or low-cost.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodge. Fayetteville is the primary gateway town with hotels, B&Bs, and vacation rentals catering to outdoor enthusiasts. The area has numerous outfitter-operated lodging options including riverside cabins and camping. Beckley (25 miles south) offers standard chain hotels.

Tips for Visitors:
- Bridge Day (third Saturday of October) is an absolutely unique event — plan well in advance as accommodations fill up months ahead.
- Book whitewater rafting trips in advance for summer weekends — outfitters on U.S. 19 near Fayetteville offer excellent guided trips.
- The Canyon Rim Visitor Center has exceptional exhibits and the staff are extremely knowledgeable about all park activities.
- Fall foliage typically peaks in mid-October — combine with Bridge Day for an unforgettable visit.
- Rock climbers should pick up a climbing guide or consult the NPS website for current access information on specific crags.
- The park is much less crowded than many national parks of similar quality — a true hidden gem for eastern outdoor enthusiasts.
