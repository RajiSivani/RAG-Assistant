Arches National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Arches National Park is located in eastern Utah near the town of Moab and was established as a national park on November 12, 1971. It covers 120 square miles (310 sq km) and contains the world's highest density of natural stone arches — over 2,000 catalogued arches ranging from 3 feet to 306 feet in span. The arches formed over millions of years as water dissolved underlying salt beds, causing the surface sandstone to crack and erode. The park also contains thousands of pinnacles, fins, and balanced rocks in shades of deep red and orange. Delicate Arch is the most recognized natural arch in the world and appears on Utah's license plates.

Location and Access:
The park is located 5 miles north of Moab, Utah on Highway 191. Moab is approximately 230 miles southeast of Salt Lake City and 110 miles from Grand Junction, Colorado. Canyonlands National Park (Needles District) is 50 miles south, making Moab an excellent base for visiting both parks. The nearest commercial airports are Grand Junction Regional Airport (110 miles) and Salt Lake City International Airport (230 miles). No public transportation serves the park — a personal vehicle is required.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $30 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $25, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $15, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.
IMPORTANT: A timed entry permit is required from April through October to enter the park between 7am and 4pm. Permits ($2 reservation fee) must be booked in advance at recreation.gov and are released 3 months in advance. They sell out very quickly.

Best Time to Visit:
Spring (March to May) is the most popular season — temperatures are pleasant (60-80°F), wildflowers bloom in April, and the red rock colors are vivid. Fall (September to November) is equally excellent with mild temperatures and beautiful light. Summer (June to August) is extremely hot — daytime temperatures regularly exceed 100°F and hiking should be avoided between 10am and 4pm. However, summer sunsets are dramatic. Winter (December to February) is cold (30-50°F) but beautiful with occasional snow on the red rock formations and minimal crowds.

Top Attractions:
1. Delicate Arch: The most iconic image in Utah — a freestanding arch 65 feet tall and 46 feet wide standing alone on the edge of a slickrock bowl with the La Sal Mountains in the background. The 3-mile round trip trail is a must-do. Best at sunset when the arch glows red-orange.
2. Landscape Arch: The longest natural arch in North America at 306 feet, located in the Devils Garden area at the end of the park road. An easy 1.9-mile hike from Devils Garden Trailhead. The arch is extremely thin and pieces have fallen in recent years.
3. Balanced Rock: A massive 3,577-ton boulder perched atop a narrow rock pedestal, visible from the road and accessed by a short 0.3-mile loop trail. One of the most photographed spots in the park.
4. Windows Section: A cluster of large arches including North Window (51 feet tall), South Window (66 feet wide), and Turret Arch, all easily accessible on a 1-mile loop trail. Double Arch — two arches sharing a common base — is also here.
5. Fiery Furnace: A labyrinthine maze of narrow sandstone fins requiring either a ranger-guided tour ($16 per person) or a permit ($10 per person) to enter independently. An intimate and adventurous experience quite unlike the open desert elsewhere.
6. Park Avenue: The first major viewpoint after the entrance — a broad sandstone canyon flanked by towering walls resembling city skyscrapers. An easy 2-mile one-way trail descends through the canyon.
7. Wolfe Ranch and Ute Rock Art Panel: Near the Delicate Arch trailhead, a preserved pioneer homestead from 1888 and a panel of Ute rock art with petroglyphs of horses and riders estimated at 150-200 years old.

Wildlife:
Despite the harsh desert environment, Arches supports surprising wildlife diversity. Animals include desert bighorn sheep (commonly seen on canyon walls), mule deer, coyotes, foxes, jackrabbits, kangaroo rats, collared lizards, rattlesnakes, and scorpions. Over 200 bird species use the park including ravens, peregrine falcons, and golden eagles. Desert cryptobiotic soil crust — a living community of cyanobacteria, lichens, and mosses — covers the ground between rocks and is critical to the desert ecosystem. Never step on it.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Balanced Rock Trail — 0.3 miles round trip, easy, quick iconic landmark.
2. Windows Loop — 1 mile round trip, easy, multiple large arches.
3. Double Arch — 0.5 miles round trip, easy.
4. Park Avenue — 2 miles one way, easy-moderate, canyon walk.
5. Delicate Arch Trail — 3 miles round trip, moderate-strenuous, the park's signature hike.
6. Broken Arch via Sand Dune Arch — 2.4 miles round trip, moderate.
7. Devils Garden Trail to Landscape Arch — 1.9 miles round trip, easy.
8. Devils Garden Primitive Loop — 7.9 miles round trip, strenuous, visits 8 arches.

Camping:
Devils Garden Campground (51 sites) is the only campground in the park, located at the end of the park road 18 miles from the entrance. It is the most popular campground in the national park system — reservations open 6 months in advance at recreation.gov and sell out immediately for all seasons. Book the day reservations open or consider staying in Moab.

Where to Stay:
There is only one campground in the park. Moab is a vibrant adventure sports town with hotels, motels, hostels, and vacation rentals for all budgets. It is the base for both Arches and Canyonlands National Parks.

Tips for Visitors:
- Book timed entry permits and campsite reservations months in advance — they are extremely competitive.
- Visit Delicate Arch at sunrise to have it to yourself and avoid the midday heat.
- Carry at least 4 liters of water per person per day in summer.
- Never walk on the black cryptobiotic soil crust — it takes 50-250 years to recover from a single footprint.
- Fiery Furnace ranger tours offer a unique intimate experience and are worth booking in advance.
- Moab is a great town to spend a day — excellent restaurants, bike rentals, and river rafting on the Colorado River.
- The park entrance road becomes one of the worst traffic jams in the national park system without a timed permit — book in advance.
