Petrified Forest National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Petrified Forest National Park is located in northeastern Arizona and was established as a national park on December 9, 1962 (originally a national monument in 1906). It covers 346 square miles (897 sq km) and protects one of the world's largest and most colorful concentrations of petrified wood — fossilized trees that lived during the Late Triassic period approximately 225 million years ago. The park also contains the Painted Desert, a vast badlands landscape of vividly colored sedimentary layers in purples, reds, oranges, and pinks, as well as hundreds of archaeological sites and extensive dinosaur fossils. The park sits at approximately 5,400 feet elevation on the Colorado Plateau.

Location and Access:
Petrified Forest is located in Navajo and Apache Counties in northeastern Arizona, approximately 25 miles east of Holbrook and 120 miles east of Flagstaff. The park is conveniently situated along Interstate 40 (the former Route 66 corridor), with entrances at the north end (Exit 311) and south end (Exit 285). U.S. Highway 180 connects the south entrance to Holbrook. The park's scenic road connects the north and south entrances (28 miles). The nearest major airports are Flagstaff Pulliam Airport (120 miles west) and Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport (200 miles southwest).

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $25 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $15, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Spring (March to May) and Fall (September to November) offer the most comfortable temperatures (55-80°F) and are considered the best times to visit. Summer (June to August) is hot (95-105°F) but the famous afternoon monsoon storms create dramatic lightning displays over the Painted Desert and cool the air in late afternoons. Sunrise and sunset are spectacular in summer after monsoon activity. Winter (December to February) is cold (temperatures can drop below freezing at night) but crowds are minimal and the park's colors are often most vivid under winter light.

Top Attractions:
1. Painted Desert Rim: The northern section of the park features sweeping overlooks of the Painted Desert — a psychedelic landscape of banded sedimentary formations in vivid purples, reds, pinks, and whites. Stops along the rim road include Tawa Point, Kachina Point, and Pintado Point. Sunrise and sunset light dramatically intensifies the colors.
2. Petrified Wood Logs: Massive petrified tree trunks — some over 6 feet in diameter — lie scattered across the landscape. The wood was silicified by mineral-rich groundwater millions of years ago, preserving the original cell structure in brilliant crystalline colors. Blue Mesa, Crystal Forest, and the Giant Logs Trail behind the Rainbow Forest Museum are must-see areas.
3. Blue Mesa: A badlands landscape of blue-gray bentonite clay hills with petrified logs jutting from eroding hillsides. The 3.5-mile Blue Mesa Loop road includes a 1-mile trail through a surreal eroded landscape.
4. Newspaper Rock: A large sandstone boulder covered with over 650 petroglyphs — symbols and images carved by ancestral Native American peoples over a period of approximately 2,000 years. One of the highest concentrations of petroglyphs in the park, viewable from an overlook.
5. Puerco Pueblo: A 100-room ancestral pueblo inhabited from about 1250 to 1380 CE, with petroglyphs on the exposed rocks. A short trail loops through the ruins.
6. Painted Desert Inn: A historic 1920s-era Route 66 inn and trading post on the Painted Desert Rim — now a National Historic Landmark and museum with exhibits on Dust Bowl-era travelers and Native American culture.

Wildlife:
The park supports abundant wildlife adapted to high desert conditions. Pronghorn, coyotes, badgers, black-tailed jackrabbits, desert cottontails, and various lizard species are commonly seen. The park is an excellent birding destination with over 200 recorded species including loggerhead shrikes, horned larks, mountain bluebirds (in winter), and numerous raptors. The park is one of the few places to see the rare Zuni bluehead sucker fish in the Puerco River.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Giant Logs Trail — 0.4 miles round trip, easy, behind the Rainbow Forest Museum, the largest logs in the park.
2. Long Logs and Agate House Trail — 2.6 miles round trip, easy, largest concentration of logs plus a partially reconstructed pueblo.
3. Blue Mesa Loop — 1 mile round trip, easy-moderate, through eroded blue badlands hills.
4. Crystal Forest Trail — 0.75 miles round trip, easy, spectacular crystallized logs.
5. Painted Desert Rim Trail — 1 mile round trip, easy, rim views and access to Painted Desert Inn.
6. Kachina Point Wilderness Trail — variable, moderate-strenuous, backcountry access into the Painted Desert wilderness.
7. Onyx Bridge Trail — 8 miles round trip, strenuous, unmarked backcountry route to a natural petrified log bridge.

Camping:
There are no developed campgrounds in Petrified Forest National Park. Backcountry camping is permitted with a free permit available at the visitor centers — backcountry campers must park at either trailhead and set up camp at least 1 mile from any road. No water is available in the backcountry. The towns of Holbrook (25 miles west) and Chambers (13 miles east) offer campgrounds and RV parks.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodge. Holbrook is the primary gateway community with several chain hotels and motels. Flagstaff (120 miles west) offers a wider range of accommodations and serves as a base for exploring the region including the Grand Canyon, Monument Valley, and Canyon de Chelly.

Tips for Visitors:
- Taking petrified wood from the park is a federal crime with serious penalties — the park actively prosecutes violators.
- Drive the entire 28-mile park road from one entrance to the other — the park changes dramatically from north to south.
- Visit Painted Desert overlooks at sunrise or sunset for the most dramatic colors.
- Bring plenty of water — the high desert heat and low humidity are dehydrating, and no water is available in the backcountry.
- The park's backcountry is one of the most unusual wilderness camping experiences in Arizona — completely flat walking through a bizarre fossil landscape.
- Summer monsoon storms (July-August) are spectacular but dangerous — lightning kills people in this open landscape every year. Return to your vehicle if storms approach.
- Combine with a stop at the Painted Desert Visitor Center (north entrance) and Rainbow Forest Museum (south entrance) for excellent geological and cultural context.
