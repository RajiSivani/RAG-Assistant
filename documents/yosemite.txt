Yosemite National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Yosemite National Park is located in the Sierra Nevada mountains of California, established in 1890. It covers 1,169 square miles (3,029 sq km) and receives approximately 4 million visitors per year, making it one of the most visited national parks in the United States. Yosemite is renowned for its dramatic granite cliffs, ancient giant sequoias, spectacular waterfalls, and diverse ecosystems ranging from oak woodlands to alpine meadows.

Location and Access:
Yosemite is located in central California, approximately 180 miles east of San Francisco and 314 miles north of Los Angeles. The park has four main entrances: Arch Rock (Highway 140, most popular), Big Oak Flat (Highway 120 west), South Entrance (Highway 41 from Fresno), and Tioga Pass (Highway 120 east, seasonal — closed in winter). The nearest airports are Fresno Yosemite International (65 miles) and San Francisco International (170 miles). YARTS bus service connects Merced, Fresno, Mammoth Lakes, and Sonora to the park.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to all national parks.
Note: A reservation system (timed entry permit) is required from late spring through summer. Check the Yosemite website for current reservation requirements before visiting.

Best Time to Visit:
Spring (April to June) is considered the best time — waterfalls are at peak flow from snowmelt, wildflowers bloom, and temperatures are pleasant (50-75°F). Summer (July to August) is the busiest season with the warmest weather, but Yosemite Valley can feel overcrowded. Fall (September to November) offers smaller crowds, golden foliage, and stable weather. Winter (December to March) is magical with snow on the granite — many facilities close but Badger Pass Ski Area opens, and the valley sees far fewer crowds.

Top Attractions:
1. Yosemite Valley: The heart of the park — a 7.5-mile-long valley carved by glaciers, surrounded by towering granite walls. Home to most of the park's iconic viewpoints, lodges, and visitor services.
2. El Capitan: A sheer granite monolith rising 3,000 feet from the valley floor. The world's most famous big wall climbing destination, made legendary by the 2017 free solo climb by Alex Honnold.
3. Half Dome: The iconic granite dome rising 8,839 feet, recognizable worldwide. The Half Dome cables hike is one of the most popular and challenging day hikes in the country — permits required.
4. Yosemite Falls: North America's tallest waterfall at 2,425 feet, consisting of three sections. Best visited in spring when snowmelt maximizes water flow.
5. Bridalveil Fall: One of the most photographed waterfalls in the park, easily accessible from a short 0.5-mile trail. The name comes from the way wind sweeps the water sideways like a veil.
6. Glacier Point: An overlook at 7,214 feet offering breathtaking panoramic views of Yosemite Valley, Half Dome, and the High Sierra. Accessible by road (summer/fall) or by strenuous hiking trails.
7. Mariposa Grove: Home to over 500 mature giant sequoias including the famous Grizzly Giant (estimated 3,000 years old) and California Tunnel Tree.
8. Tuolumne Meadows: At 8,600 feet elevation, this is the largest subalpine meadow in the Sierra Nevada, offering excellent hiking, rock climbing, and wildlife watching.

Wildlife:
Yosemite supports over 400 species of vertebrates. Common animals include black bears (approximately 300-500 in the park), mule deer, coyotes, bobcats, mountain lions, peregrine falcons, great gray owls, and over 260 species of birds. All food must be stored in bear-proof containers or lockers — bears that obtain human food are often euthanized.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Mirror Lake Loop — 5 miles, easy, beautiful reflection of Half Dome.
2. Mist Trail to Vernal Fall — 3 miles round trip, moderate, stunning waterfall views.
3. Mist Trail to Nevada Fall — 7 miles round trip, strenuous, two spectacular waterfalls.
4. Half Dome Trail — 14-16 miles round trip, extremely strenuous, requires permit ($10), involves climbing steel cables.
5. Panorama Trail — 8.5 miles one way from Glacier Point, moderate-strenuous.
6. Four Mile Trail — 9.6 miles round trip to Glacier Point, strenuous.
7. Valley Floor Loop — 13 miles, easy, loops entire Yosemite Valley.

Camping:
Yosemite has 13 campgrounds with approximately 1,400 sites. Valley campgrounds (Upper Pines, Lower Pines, North Pines) require advance reservations booked through recreation.gov — these fill up within minutes when released 5 months in advance. Hodgdon Meadow, Crane Flat, and Tuolumne Meadows campgrounds are also reservable. White Wolf and Yosemite Creek are first-come, first-served.

Where to Stay:
The Ahwahnee (formerly Yosemite Valley Lodge) is a historic luxury hotel in the valley — one of the most iconic hotels in America. Yosemite Valley Lodge, Curry Village (Half Dome Village), and Housekeeping Camp offer mid-range options. All in-park lodging is managed by Aramark and must be booked many months in advance, especially for summer.

Tips for Visitors:
- Use the free valley shuttle system instead of driving — parking in the valley is extremely limited.
- Book Half Dome permits as far in advance as possible — they sell out immediately.
- Download the Yosemite app for offline maps and real-time shuttle tracking.
- Visit Tunnel View at sunrise for the iconic valley panorama with minimal crowds.
- Bears are extremely active — never leave food in your car, even for minutes.
- Tioga Road (Highway 120 over the Sierra) is typically closed November through May or June.
