Glacier Bay National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Glacier Bay National Park and Preserve is located in southeastern Alaska and was established as a national park on December 2, 1980. It covers 5,130 square miles (13,287 sq km), making it one of the largest national parks in the United States. The park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is famous for its dramatic tidewater glaciers, fjords, and one of the world's most impressive examples of glacial retreat and ecological succession. Just 250 years ago, the entire bay was covered by a massive glacier — today, the bay stretches 65 miles, revealing a landscape that is being rapidly colonized by plants and animals.

Location and Access:
Glacier Bay is located in the Alaska Panhandle, approximately 50 miles west of Juneau. There is no road access to the park — it is accessible only by air or sea. Small planes fly daily between Juneau and Gustavus (the gateway community near the park). The Alaska Marine Highway provides ferry service to Gustavus. Most visitors arrive via cruise ship — the park is one of the most popular cruise destinations in Alaska, with ships entering the bay from late May through early September. The nearest major airport is Juneau International Airport.

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for Glacier Bay National Park.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to all national parks.
Note: Cruise ship and charter vessel operators pay fees to enter the bay. Private boaters and kayakers must obtain a free permit to enter Glacier Bay from June 1 through August 31 — these are available at the park visitor center in Bartlett Cove.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (June to August) is the primary visitor season, with the best weather, longest days (up to 18+ hours of daylight), and most wildlife activity. Temperatures typically range from 45-65°F. July and August offer the best chances of seeing calving glaciers and peak whale activity. May and September are shoulder months with fewer crowds but more unpredictable weather. Winter brings severe weather, near-darkness, and most services close — only the most experienced wilderness travelers visit then.

Top Attractions:
1. Glacier Viewing: The upper bay contains numerous active tidewater glaciers including Margerie Glacier and Grand Pacific Glacier. Margerie Glacier is particularly active, regularly calving massive icebergs into the bay with thunderous booms. Tour boats and cruise ships bring visitors close to these glaciers.
2. Humpback Whale Watching: Glacier Bay is one of the premier humpback whale feeding grounds in Alaska. Whales arrive in late spring and summer to feed on the abundant herring and krill. Whale watching from tour boats and kayaks is extraordinary.
3. Sea Kayaking: Paddling through Glacier Bay is considered one of the great wilderness kayaking experiences in the world. Kayakers can explore remote coves, observe wildlife from water level, and access areas inaccessible to larger vessels. Multi-day kayak camping expeditions are popular.
4. Bartlett Cove: The park's only developed area, featuring the park lodge, visitor center, campground, and the Bartlett River and Forest Loop trails. The Bartlett Cove area represents a mature Sitka spruce-hemlock forest — the endpoint of ecological succession following glacial retreat.
5. Wildlife Watching: The park is home to prolific wildlife including brown and black bears, mountain goats, wolves, Steller sea lions, harbor seals, Dall's porpoises, and orcas. Seabird colonies are extensive, with murrelets, puffins, kittiwakes, and cormorants.
6. Fairweather Range: The towering St. Elias Mountains, including Mount Fairweather (15,325 feet) on the park's western border, provide a spectacular backdrop visible on clear days.

Wildlife:
Glacier Bay supports exceptional marine and terrestrial biodiversity. Humpback whales, orcas, Dall's porpoises, harbor porpoises, Steller sea lions, and harbor seals are regularly seen. Brown bears and black bears frequent the shorelines. Mountain goats cling to steep cliffs. Wolves roam the wilderness. The park has over 240 bird species including tufted puffins, marbled murrelets, bald eagles, and Arctic terns that migrate from Antarctica.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Bartlett River Trail — 8 miles round trip, easy-moderate, through mature forest with excellent birding and bear viewing.
2. Forest Loop Trail — 1 mile round trip, easy, through old-growth Sitka spruce near Bartlett Cove.
3. Bartlett Lake Trail — 4 miles round trip, moderate, to a quiet backcountry lake.
4. Point Gustavus Beach Walk — variable distance, easy, along the beach to the park boundary and back.
Note: Most of the park is designated wilderness without maintained trails — backcountry travel is cross-country and requires excellent wilderness navigation skills.

Camping:
Bartlett Cove Campground (25 sites, free) is the only developed campground, operated on a first-come, first-served basis. Sites include bear boxes and bear-proof food storage is required. Backcountry camping throughout the park is permitted with no fees but requires a free permit from June through August. All backcountry campers must attend a required orientation at the visitor center before departure.

Where to Stay:
Glacier Bay Lodge at Bartlett Cove is the only in-park lodging — a comfortable wilderness lodge open from late May through early September. Book well in advance as it fills quickly. Gustavus (8 miles from the park) offers several charming bed-and-breakfasts and small lodges including Gustavus Inn, known for excellent meals featuring local seafood and garden produce.

Tips for Visitors:
- If arriving by cruise ship, book a kayak or small boat tour in advance through the park's authorized concessionaires.
- Bear encounters are common along shorelines — carry bear spray and know how to use it.
- Kayakers must attend the required orientation before entering the bay — plan time for this.
- Weather changes rapidly — always carry rain gear and dress in waterproof layers.
- Bring binoculars for wildlife and glacier viewing — they make an enormous difference.
- Book Glacier Bay Lodge and Gustavus accommodations 6-12 months in advance for summer visits.
- The free daily ranger programs aboard cruise ships and at the visitor center are excellent.
