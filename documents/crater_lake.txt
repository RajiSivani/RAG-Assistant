Crater Lake National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Crater Lake National Park is located in the Cascade Mountains of southern Oregon and was established as a national park on May 22, 1902 — the fifth national park in the United States. It covers 286 square miles (741 sq km). The park's centerpiece is Crater Lake — the deepest lake in the United States at 1,943 feet (592 meters) and one of the deepest in the world. The lake was formed approximately 7,700 years ago when the massive volcano Mount Mazama (over 12,000 feet tall) erupted catastrophically and collapsed inward, forming the caldera that eventually filled with rainwater and snowmelt over hundreds of years. The result is a lake of legendary clarity and an almost impossible shade of deep blue — one of the most beautiful natural sights in America.

Location and Access:
Crater Lake is located in southern Oregon, approximately 80 miles north of Klamath Falls and 85 miles northeast of Medford. The park is accessed via OR-62 (south and west approaches) or OR-138 (north approach). The Rim Village area on the south rim is the main visitor hub. The nearest major airports are Rogue Valley-Medford International (85 miles) and Klamath Falls Airport (80 miles). Portland is 280 miles north. The North Entrance and Rim Drive are typically closed from late October through late June due to snow — the South Entrance via OR-62 remains open year-round.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (July to August) is the prime season — the full 33-mile Rim Drive is open, boat tours operate to Wizard Island, and all facilities are available. Wildflowers bloom across the caldera rim. Temperatures are mild (60-75°F) with occasional thunderstorms. Fall (September to October) brings fewer crowds and the possibility of the first snowfall dusting the rim dramatically. Winter and Spring (November to June): The park receives an average of 44 feet of snow per year — the snow record is 879 inches (73 feet) in one season. The lake never freezes but is surrounded by deep snow. The rim road is closed but the park is open for snowshoeing and cross-country skiing — a magnificent winter experience.

Top Attractions:
1. Rim Drive: A 33-mile road circling the entire caldera rim with over 30 pullouts and overlooks. Each viewpoint reveals a different perspective of the impossibly blue lake below. Cloudcap is the highest point (8,070 feet). Allow 3-4 hours to complete the full loop.
2. Wizard Island: A cinder cone rising 763 feet above the lake surface — a volcano within a volcano. Accessible by concessionaire boat tour from Cleetwood Cove Trail, with options for hiking to the summit or snorkeling in the extraordinarily clear water.
3. Cleetwood Cove Trail: The only legal access to the lake shore — a strenuous 1.1-mile trail (2.2 miles round trip) descending 700 feet to the water. Boat tour tickets are purchased and boarded here.
4. Watchman Peak: A short but rewarding 1.6-mile round trip hike to a fire lookout tower offering one of the best panoramic views of the lake and Wizard Island, and the surrounding Cascade peaks.
5. Crater Lake Lodge: Built in 1915, this historic grand lodge sits dramatically on the rim edge with unobstructed views of the lake. The lobby and dining room are open to non-guests. Staying here for a sunrise over the lake is a bucket-list experience.
6. The Phantom Ship: A jagged rock formation rising from the lake that resembles a ghost ship — best seen from the east rim viewpoints.
7. Sun Notch Viewpoint: One of the best viewpoints for photographing the Phantom Ship, reached by a short 0.4-mile trail.

Wildlife:
The park supports black bears, mountain lions, mule deer, porcupines, chipmunks, Clark's nutcrackers, and Steller's jays. The lake itself is uniquely inhabited — rainbow trout and kokanee salmon were introduced in the late 1800s and can be fished. The native Mazama newt is found only in Crater Lake. Osprey, bald eagles, and peregrine falcons hunt over the lake. Elk are seen in the meadows and forest surrounding the park.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Watchman Peak Trail — 1.6 miles round trip, moderate, fire lookout with best lake views.
2. Cleetwood Cove Trail — 2.2 miles round trip, strenuous, only access to lake level.
3. Garfield Peak Trail — 3.4 miles round trip, strenuous, dramatic summit views.
4. Discovery Point Trail — 2.8 miles round trip, easy, along rim to discovery viewpoint.
5. Mount Scott Trail — 5 miles round trip, strenuous, highest point in park (8,929 feet).
6. The Pinnacles Trail — 2 miles round trip, easy, unique spire formations.
7. Pacific Crest Trail — passes through the park's western edge, through old-growth forest.

Camping:
Mazama Campground (214 sites) near the South Entrance is the main campground, open mid-June through October, reservable at recreation.gov. Lost Creek Campground (16 tent-only sites) on the east rim is first-come, first-served. Backcountry camping requires a free permit from the visitor center.

Where to Stay:
Crater Lake Lodge on the rim is one of the most iconic park lodges in America — reserve a year in advance for peak summer dates. Mazama Village Cabins offer more affordable in-park options. Klamath Falls and Medford provide wider accommodation options.

Tips for Visitors:
- Book boat tours to Wizard Island as soon as tickets are released — they sell out within hours.
- Crater Lake Lodge reservations open roughly a year in advance and fill immediately for summer.
- The lake is at 6,178 feet elevation — expect cool temperatures even in summer and bring layers.
- Driving the full 33-mile Rim Drive is essential — don't skip the less-visited east and north rim overlooks.
- The lake water is exceptionally cold (33-55°F) — swimming is permitted at Cleetwood Cove but brief.
- Arrive at sunrise for the most dramatic light on the caldera walls and fewest crowds.
- If visiting in early or late season, call ahead — the rim road opens and closes depending on snowplowing.
