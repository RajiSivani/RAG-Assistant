Indiana Dunes National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Indiana Dunes National Park is located along the southern shore of Lake Michigan in northwestern Indiana and was established as a national park on February 15, 2019 (previously a national lakeshore since 1966). It covers 24 square miles (62 sq km). The park protects 15 miles of Lake Michigan shoreline, towering sand dunes reaching up to 200 feet, wetlands, prairies, bogs, river floodplains, and historic farmsteads. Despite being located in the industrial region of northwest Indiana between Chicago and Gary, the park hosts one of the most biologically diverse assemblages of plant species in the national park system — over 1,100 plant species due to the convergence of multiple ecosystems at this unique geographic crossroads. The dunes were formed by glacial deposits and continue to shift through wind action.

Location and Access:
Indiana Dunes is located in Porter and LaPorte Counties, Indiana, along the Lake Michigan shoreline. The park is approximately 50 miles east of Chicago and 25 miles west of South Bend. The Dorothy Buell Memorial Visitor Center in Porter is the main hub. Remarkably, the park is served by public transportation — the South Shore Line commuter rail connects Chicago's Millennium Station to Beverly Shores, Dune Park, and other stops within the park boundary. The nearest major airport is Chicago O'Hare International (55 miles).

Entry Fees:
The park is free to enter.
Note: A fee ($6/vehicle) is charged for parking at certain beach areas during peak summer season.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (June to August) is peak season for beach activities — the Lake Michigan beaches are excellent for swimming (though the lake stays cool: 65-70°F) and the dune climbing is most popular. This is the busiest season. Fall (September to October) is excellent for hiking and leaf-peeping — crowds thin dramatically and the varied forest provides beautiful foliage. Spring (April to May) brings wildflowers and migratory birds in abundance — excellent for birding. Winter (December to February) transforms the dunes into a surreal, peaceful landscape — frozen Lake Michigan creates stunning ice formations along the shore.

Top Attractions:
1. Mount Baldy: The park's most famous dune — a 126-foot "living dune" that has been slowly migrating toward a forest due to the loss of stabilizing vegetation. The climb is strenuous but the view of Lake Michigan from the top is breathtaking. Guided tours are required to protect the fragile ecosystem (limited entry).
2. West Beach: A wide, beautiful beach with facilities and a boardwalk — the most developed and popular swimming beach in the park. Dune climbing permitted at the adjacent blowout dune.
3. Cowles Bog Trail: A 4.7-mile loop through one of the most scientifically significant landscapes in the park — a quaking bog, dunes, and woodlands that inspired early plant ecologist Henry Cowles's groundbreaking theory of ecological succession in 1899.
4. Dune Succession Trail: A 1.4-mile trail that demonstrates the ecological succession from open lake shore to mature forest — the plant communities change dramatically across just a quarter mile. Educational and ecologically fascinating.
5. Paul H. Douglas Center for Environmental Education: A modern facility offering exhibits on the park's ecology and history, ranger programs, and access to the Miller Woods trail system — excellent for families.
6. Portage Lakefront and Riverwalk: A developed beach with a fishing pier offering the best views of Chicago's skyline from inside a national park — a uniquely industrial-yet-beautiful scene.
7. The 1933 Century of Progress Homes: Five historic homes from Chicago's 1933 World's Fair were transported to Beverly Shores and remain preserved in the park — an unusual piece of 20th-century American history.

Wildlife:
Indiana Dunes is an exceptional birding destination — the park records over 350 bird species due to its location on the Lake Michigan shoreline along the Mississippi Flyway. Migrating raptors, shorebirds, waterfowl, and songbirds pass through in enormous numbers in spring and fall. Breeding birds include sandhill cranes, great blue herons, great egrets, osprey, cerulean warblers, and yellow-breasted chats. Mammals include white-tailed deer, red foxes, coyotes, and minks along the rivers.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Dune Succession Trail — 1.4 miles loop, moderate, ecological education.
2. Cowles Bog Trail — 4.7 miles loop, moderate, bog and dune diversity.
3. Heron Rookery Trail — 4.4 miles loop, easy-moderate, Little Calumet River, great blue herons.
4. Ly-co-ki-we Trail — 3-10 miles (various routes), easy-moderate, mixed forest and prairie.
5. Tolleston Dunes Trail — 3.8 miles loop, moderate, oak savannas and wetlands.
6. West Beach Dune Succession Walk — self-guided, 0.4 miles.

Camping:
Dunewood Campground (66 sites) is the only in-park campground — available with advance reservations at recreation.gov. It is not on the beach but provides access to trails and facilities.

Where to Stay:
The gateway communities of Porter, Chesterton, and Michigan City have numerous hotels and bed-and-breakfasts. The charming town of Chesterton is particularly attractive. Many visitors make the park a day trip from Chicago, an hour away by train or car.

Tips for Visitors:
- Take the South Shore Line from Chicago for a completely car-free visit — arrive at Dune Park station, walk to the beach.
- Rip currents can be dangerous in Lake Michigan — always swim at beaches with lifeguards on duty.
- Mount Baldy requires a guided tour reservation — book at the visitor center.
- The park's bird diversity is extraordinary — a birding checklist and binoculars are worthwhile.
- The beach is accessible year-round — winter frozen dune landscapes are magical.
- Parking can be limited at popular beach areas on summer weekends — arrive early or use the train.
- The park is surrounded by active industrial areas but the natural areas within are genuinely wild and beautiful.
