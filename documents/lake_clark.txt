Lake Clark National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Lake Clark National Park and Preserve is located in southwestern Alaska and was established on December 2, 1980. It covers 6,297 square miles (16,309 sq km) and is one of the least visited national parks in the country, receiving fewer than 20,000 visitors annually. The park encompasses an extraordinary range of landscapes — from the volcanic peaks of the Alaska Range and Aleutian Range (including two active volcanoes), to pristine rivers, large lakes, boreal forest, and a wild Pacific coastline on Cook Inlet. Lake Clark is particularly renowned for brown bear viewing along the coast, exceptional sport fishing, and true wilderness solitude.

Location and Access:
Lake Clark is located approximately 100 miles southwest of Anchorage, across Cook Inlet. There is absolutely no road access — the park is accessible only by small aircraft or boat. Most visitors fly by small plane or float plane from Anchorage or Kenai to Port Alsworth (the main park hub on Lake Clark) or directly to bear viewing areas like Chinitna Bay or Crescent Lake. Several air taxi services operate from Anchorage. The flight from Anchorage takes approximately 1 hour in a small aircraft.

Entry Fees:
There is no entrance fee for Lake Clark National Park.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — covers entrance to all national parks.

Best Time to Visit:
Summer (June to mid-September) is the only practical visitor season. June and July are best for brown bear viewing as bears congregate on beaches foraging for clams and sedge grass before salmon runs begin. July and August are prime for salmon fishing and bear-salmon viewing. August brings peak wildflowers in the alpine zones. Late August to September brings spectacular fall colors and peak salmon runs. The park receives significant rainfall — rain gear is essential. Temperatures range from 50-70°F in summer.

Top Attractions:
1. Brown Bear Viewing at Chinitna Bay: The tidal flats and sedge meadows around Chinitna Bay on the Cook Inlet coast attract large concentrations of brown bears, particularly in early summer (June-July) when bears feast on clams and sedge grass. The beach-based viewing experience is more intimate than some other Alaska bear destinations. Many guides offer day trips from Anchorage.
2. Brown Bear Viewing at Crescent Lake: Bears gather at Crescent Lake during salmon runs in July and August. Float plane access brings visitors directly to viewing areas where bears can be observed fishing for sockeye salmon.
3. Tuxedni Bay and Tuxedni Wilderness: A remote coastal wilderness with dramatic scenery, outstanding sea kayaking, seabird colonies, and excellent wildlife viewing. The area is largely unvisited and offers complete solitude.
4. Lake Clark Itself: The park's namesake — a 42-mile-long glacially carved lake surrounded by boreal forest and mountains. Sport fishing for sockeye salmon, rainbow trout, Arctic grayling, and northern pike is exceptional.
5. Redoubt Volcano and Iliamna Volcano: Two active stratovolcanoes dominating the park's skyline. Redoubt erupted most recently in 2009. Both volcanoes are visible from much of the park and Cook Inlet.
6. River Floating and Kayaking: The Mulchatna, Tlikakila, and Chilikadrotna Rivers offer outstanding wilderness float trips through remote river valleys. These multi-day expeditions are among the most spectacular in Alaska.

Wildlife:
Lake Clark supports remarkable wildlife. Brown bears are the signature animal — the coastal areas host significant populations. Moose, caribou (Mulchatna herd), wolves, wolverines, and Dall's sheep inhabit the park. The Cook Inlet coastline supports beluga whales, harbor seals, sea otters, and Steller sea lions. The park's rivers and lakes support all five species of Pacific salmon. Over 190 bird species have been recorded including bald eagles, golden eagles, peregrine falcons, and numerous shorebird and waterfowl species.

Top Hiking Trails:
There are no maintained trails in Lake Clark National Park. All hiking is cross-country over tundra, ridges, and river bars. The terrain around Port Alsworth and in the alpine zones above the lakes offers excellent cross-country hiking. Route-finding and wilderness navigation skills are essential. The coastline can be walked along beaches when tides permit.

Camping:
There are no developed campgrounds in the park. A few public-use cabins are available on Lake Clark and can be reserved through recreation.gov. All other camping is dispersed wilderness camping. Bear-proof food canisters are required and essential given the high bear density. The small community of Port Alsworth has the Wilder Place Lodge and Farm Lodge offering modest accommodations and meals.

Where to Stay:
Port Alsworth (accessible only by small plane) serves as the main hub with a few wilderness lodges. Several private wilderness lodges operate within and adjacent to the park, offering all-inclusive bear viewing, fishing, and wilderness packages — these are typically expensive but provide a complete experience. Day trips from Anchorage to bear viewing areas via air taxi are popular.

Tips for Visitors:
- This park is primarily accessed through guided tours and wilderness lodges — independent travel requires serious planning and experience.
- Contact the park visitor center in Port Alsworth for current bear activity information and air taxi recommendations.
- Bear safety is paramount — attend a bear safety briefing before any independent backcountry travel.
- Budget realistically — flights, guides, and lodging in this remote area are expensive but the experience is unique.
- Day trips from Anchorage for bear viewing are available from multiple guide services and are worth the cost.
- A satellite communicator is essential safety equipment — cell service does not exist in the park.
- Layered rain gear and rubber boots are essential for coastal bear viewing areas.
