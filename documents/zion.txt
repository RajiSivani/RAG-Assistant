Zion National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Zion National Park is located in southwestern Utah and was established as a national park in 1919. It covers 229 square miles (593 sq km) and is Utah's first and most visited national park, attracting over 4.5 million visitors annually. Zion is famous for its steep red and white sandstone cliffs, narrow slot canyons, and the Virgin River that carved the canyon over millions of years. The park's name comes from a Hebrew word meaning "sanctuary" or "refuge."

Location and Access:
Zion is located near Springdale, Utah, approximately 160 miles northeast of Las Vegas, Nevada and 310 miles south of Salt Lake City. The main South Entrance is accessed through the town of Springdale. The East Entrance is reached via Highway 9 through the Zion-Mount Carmel Tunnel — vehicles over 7'10" wide or 11'4" tall require an escort and special fee. The nearest major airport is Las Vegas Harry Reid International (160 miles), though St. George Regional Airport is only 45 miles away.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80.
Note: Private vehicles are not allowed on Zion Canyon Scenic Drive from April through November — visitors must use the free park shuttle system.

Best Time to Visit:
Spring (March to May) is the most popular time — waterfalls from snowmelt, wildflowers, and pleasant temperatures (55-80°F). Slot canyons carry high flash flood risk in spring. Summer (June to August) is hot (95-105°F in the canyon) but the Virgin River Narrows wading hike is at its most accessible. Afternoon thunderstorms increase flash flood risk July-September. Fall (September to November) is arguably the best season — comfortable temperatures, fewer crowds than spring, golden cottonwood trees, and clearer skies. Winter (December to February) is cold but stunning with snow-dusted red rocks and minimal crowds — some trails may be icy.

Top Attractions:
1. Angels Landing: One of the most famous and thrilling hikes in America. A 5.4-mile round trip trail that ends with a steep half-mile chain-assisted scramble to a narrow 1,488-foot summit with breathtaking 360-degree views. A permit is now required for the final chain section ($6 per person). Not recommended for those with a fear of heights.
2. The Narrows: A world-famous hike through the Virgin River itself, with canyon walls towering 1,000 feet above and sometimes only 20 feet apart. The bottom-up route starts at the Temple of Sinawava shuttle stop. Rental of neoprene socks, water shoes, and walking sticks is strongly recommended. Closed when water levels are too high.
3. Emerald Pools Trails: Three pools connected by trails at varying difficulty levels. Lower Emerald Pool (1.2 miles, easy), Middle Emerald Pool (2.2 miles, moderate), Upper Emerald Pool (3 miles, strenuous). Waterfalls and hanging gardens make this a stunning route.
4. Canyon Overlook Trail: A 1-mile round trip moderate hike offering spectacular views of Zion Canyon. One of the best bang-for-buck short hikes in the park.
5. Weeping Rock: A short 0.4-mile round trip hike to a spring-fed alcove where water seeps through the sandstone and drips from above. Hanging gardens of ferns and wildflowers flourish here.
6. Kolob Canyons: A less-visited section of the park in the northwest corner, accessible from I-15. Features finger canyons of deep red rock and leads to the world's largest natural arch — Kolob Arch (287 feet span).

Wildlife:
Zion supports over 290 bird species including the rare California condor, Mexican spotted owl, and peregrine falcon. Mammals include mule deer, desert bighorn sheep, ringtail cats, coyotes, bobcats, and mountain lions. The park has 30 species of reptiles including the Great Basin rattlesnake. Mule deer are very common and often seen near the visitor center.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Riverside Walk — 2.2 miles round trip, easy, paved, leads to Narrows entry point.
2. Canyon Overlook Trail — 1 mile round trip, moderate, great views for minimal effort.
3. Emerald Pools (Lower) — 1.2 miles round trip, easy.
4. Angels Landing — 5.4 miles round trip, strenuous, chains required, permit needed.
5. The Narrows (bottom-up) — up to 9 miles round trip, moderate-strenuous, wading required.
6. West Rim Trail — 14.2 miles one way, strenuous backcountry route.
7. Observation Point — 8 miles round trip, strenuous, offers views looking DOWN at Angels Landing.

Camping:
Watchman Campground (309 sites, electric hookups available) accepts reservations at recreation.gov. South Campground (117 sites) is first-come, first-served from October to March, reservable rest of year. Lava Point Campground (6 sites) is primitive, first-come, first-served, and open June to October.

Where to Stay:
Zion Lodge is the only in-park accommodation — a historic lodge in the heart of Zion Canyon with stunning views. Book 6+ months in advance. Springdale, the gateway town just outside the park entrance, offers dozens of hotels, B&Bs, and vacation rentals at various price points.

Tips for Visitors:
- Park your car in Springdale and take the free town shuttle to the park entrance — saves enormous hassle.
- Book Angels Landing permits well in advance through recreation.gov.
- Always check flash flood forecasts before entering the Narrows or any slot canyon.
- Rent Narrows gear from one of the outfitters in Springdale the evening before your hike.
- Arrive before 8am for popular trailheads in spring and summer — shuttles get very crowded.
- The Zion-Mount Carmel Highway (Highway 9) driving through the tunnel is a spectacular scenic route itself.
