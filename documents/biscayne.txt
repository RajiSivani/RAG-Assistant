Biscayne National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Biscayne National Park is located in southern Florida and was established as a national park on June 28, 1980 (previously a national monument since 1968). It covers 270 square miles (700 sq km), of which 95% is water — making it one of the most unique national parks in the United States. The park protects the northernmost Florida Keys, a pristine stretch of Biscayne Bay, and a portion of the third-largest coral reef system in the world. It lies just south of Miami, yet remains remarkably wild and undeveloped. The park preserves a mosaic of four distinct ecosystems: the shallow waters of Biscayne Bay, the keys' mangrove shorelines, the upland habitats of the keys, and the offshore coral reefs.

Location and Access:
Biscayne is located at the southern tip of Florida, approximately 25 miles south of Miami and 45 miles north of Key West. The Dante Fascell Visitor Center at Convoy Point is the mainland access point, reachable via SW 328th Street off US-1 in Homestead. Crucially, there is no road access to the keys or coral reefs within the park — all access beyond the mainland is by boat. Visitors without their own boats must use park concession boats for tours. Miami International Airport is approximately 40 miles north.

Entry Fees:
The park is free to enter at the mainland visitor center.
Boat launch fee: $25 per day at the Convoy Point boat ramp.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 (covers the boat launch fee).

Best Time to Visit:
Winter (December to April) is the best season — dry season weather means clear skies, calm seas, reduced mosquitoes, and comfortable temperatures (65-80°F). This is the peak season for snorkeling, diving, and exploring the keys. Summer (May to August) brings the wet season with daily afternoon thunderstorms, high humidity, heavy mosquitoes, and the possibility of hurricanes. Fall (September to November) is hurricane season — check conditions before any boat trips. Water visibility for snorkeling and diving is generally best January through April.

Top Attractions:
1. Coral Reefs: The park's crown jewel — a 4-mile-wide, 18-mile-long tract of living coral reef with over 500 species of fish, sea turtles, manatees, dolphins, and a stunning variety of corals. Accessible by snorkel or scuba from park concession boats.
2. Elliott Key: The largest island in the park, accessible by boat. Features hiking trails through tropical hardwood hammock forest, a visitor center, campground, and swimming beach. The "spite highway" — a road bulldozed by developers before the park was created — bisects the island.
3. Adams Key: A small island with a picnic area and short nature trail through dense tropical vegetation. Used to host a private sportfishing club frequented by U.S. presidents.
4. Boca Chita Key: The most popular destination in the park, featuring an ornamental lighthouse, seawall, and picnic grounds. The 65-foot lighthouse was built by a private owner in the 1930s and was never approved for navigation — a quirky and photogenic landmark.
5. Maritime Heritage Trail: An underwater trail connecting six historic shipwrecks within the park, ranging from a 19th-century sailing vessel to a 20th-century cargo ship — a world-class scuba diving experience.
6. Mangrove Kayaking: Paddling through the tangled mangrove shorelines of the keys offers an intimate wildlife experience — look for crocodiles (yes, American crocodiles), manatees, and nesting birds.

Wildlife:
Biscayne supports exceptional marine and coastal wildlife. The coral reefs host over 500 fish species, sea turtles (loggerhead, green, leatherback), nurse sharks, reef sharks, lobsters, and rays. Manatees (West Indian) are commonly seen in the bay. The American crocodile — one of the rarest reptiles in North America — nests in the park. Bottlenose dolphins frequent the bay year-round. The keys host over 170 bird species including ospreys, brown pelicans, frigatebirds, and roseate spoonbills.

Top Activities:
1. Snorkeling — Join a concession boat tour to the reefs; equipment rental available.
2. Scuba Diving — Explore the Maritime Heritage Trail shipwrecks; certification required.
3. Glass-Bottom Boat Tours — See the reef without getting wet on a narrated boat tour.
4. Kayaking and Canoeing — Paddle through mangroves; rentals available from the visitor center.
5. Fishing — Excellent saltwater fishing in the bay; Florida fishing license required.
6. Island Camping — Boat camping on Elliott Key or Boca Chita Key offers a unique wilderness experience.

Camping:
Elliott Key Campground (40 sites) and Boca Chita Key Campground (no fixed sites — limited to 25 boats) are accessible only by boat. Sites have no freshwater — you must bring all water. Reservations are available at recreation.gov. Campfires are not permitted.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodging. The nearby city of Homestead and the Florida Keys (accessible via US-1) offer numerous hotel and motel options. Miami Beach and Miami are 40 miles north and provide the widest range of lodging.

Tips for Visitors:
- You cannot experience the best of this park without a boat — book concession tours in advance, especially in winter.
- Always check NOAA weather forecasts before heading out on the water — afternoon thunderstorms develop rapidly.
- Mosquitoes can be overwhelming near the mangroves — bring strong insect repellent.
- Sunscreen and sun protection are essential — the Florida sun on open water is intense.
- The park's reefs are protected — do not touch, stand on, or collect any coral or marine life.
- American crocodiles, while rarely aggressive, should be given a wide berth — do not feed or approach them.
- The park is adjacent to Everglades National Park — combine both for a full south Florida wilderness experience.
