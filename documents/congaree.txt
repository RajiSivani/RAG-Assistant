Congaree National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Congaree National Park is located in central South Carolina and was established as a national park on November 10, 2003 (previously a national monument since 1976). It covers 41 square miles (107 sq km) and protects the largest intact expanse of old-growth bottomland hardwood forest remaining in the southeastern United States. The park contains extraordinarily tall trees — more state and national champion trees (by height) than any other place in North America — creating a lush cathedral-like canopy up to 170 feet high. The Congaree and Wateree Rivers regularly flood the forest, depositing nutrients that support this remarkable ecosystem. Congaree is an International Biosphere Reserve and a Ramsar Wetland of International Importance. It is one of the least-visited national parks yet one of the most ecologically significant.

Location and Access:
Congaree is located in central South Carolina, approximately 20 miles southeast of Columbia via SC-48 (Bluff Road). The Harry Hampton Visitor Center at the north end of the park is the main entry point. Columbia Metropolitan Airport (20 miles) and Charlotte Douglas International Airport (95 miles) are the nearest major airports. The park is easily visited as a day trip from Columbia.

Entry Fees:
The park is free to enter.

Best Time to Visit:
Fall (October to November) and Spring (March to April) are the best times — comfortable temperatures (55-75°F), lower water levels for hiking, fewer insects, and stunning light through the canopy. The synchronous firefly event in late May to early June is a world-famous spectacle (timed lottery tickets required). Summer (June to August) is hot (85-95°F), extremely humid, and mosquitoes are voracious — bring maximum insect repellent and consider it a last resort. Winter (December to February) is quiet, cool, and has the fewest insects — a surprisingly pleasant time to walk the boardwalk.

Top Attractions:
1. Boardwalk Loop: The park's signature trail — a 2.4-mile (or 4.4-mile extended) elevated boardwalk that winds through the floodplain forest, passing massive champion trees, oxbow lakes, and over Cedar Creek. The boardwalk is accessible and allows visitors to experience the forest interior without getting muddy. After heavy rain, the forest floor floods and the boardwalk becomes even more magical.
2. Synchronous Fireflies: From late May to early June, Photinus carolinus fireflies synchronize their light flashes in waves across the darkened forest — one of only a handful of places in the world where this occurs. The park holds a lottery for vehicle passes to access the firefly viewing area due to overwhelming demand. This event rivals Gatlinburg's firefly festival in Great Smoky Mountains.
3. Cedar Creek Canoe Trail: A 15-mile marked canoe trail through the park's backcountry along Cedar Creek — an intimate way to experience the flooded forest from water level. Canoes and kayaks can be rented from local outfitters outside the park.
4. Weston Lake Loop: A 4.5-mile backcountry trail looping through the floodplain to Weston Lake — an oxbow lake remnant of the Congaree River. Excellent for birdwatching.
5. Champion Trees: The park contains an extraordinary number of champion trees. The national champion loblolly pine (167 feet tall) and state champion water hickory, common persimmon, and many others tower above the floodplain.
6. Birdwatching: Congaree is one of the top birding sites in South Carolina, with over 200 species recorded including prothonotary warblers, Swainson's warblers, red-headed woodpeckers, barred owls, and wood ducks.

Wildlife:
The park's floodplain forests support river otters, bobcats, white-tailed deer, wild boar, and American alligators in the waterways. Over 200 bird species are recorded. The park is famous for its synchronous fireflies and has an abundance of other insects and reptiles — cottonmouth water moccasins are present and should be respected. The creek and river systems support a variety of turtles, including river cooters and box turtles.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Boardwalk Loop (Short) — 2.4 miles loop, easy, fully accessible.
2. Boardwalk Loop (Extended) — 4.4 miles loop, easy, reaches deeper forest.
3. Weston Lake Loop — 4.5 miles loop, easy-moderate, through backcountry floodplain.
4. River Trail — 10 miles one way, strenuous backcountry, follows Congaree River.
5. Oakridge Trail — 6.3 miles round trip, moderate.

Camping:
Longleaf Campground (10 sites) is a drive-in, first-come, first-served primitive campground with water and vault toilets. Bluff Campground is a backcountry walk-in site (1.6 miles from trailhead). Both are free. Backcountry camping is also allowed throughout the park with a free permit from the visitor center. Bear boxes are not required but food must be hung or stored properly.

Where to Stay:
There is no in-park lodging. Columbia, 20 miles north, offers all major hotel chains and numerous restaurants. The park is easily explored as a day trip from Columbia.

Tips for Visitors:
- Insect repellent is essential from April through October — mosquitoes here are legendary in their intensity.
- Apply for the synchronous firefly lottery early — it opens months before the event and is highly competitive.
- Water levels fluctuate dramatically — check the park website for current conditions before hiking backcountry trails.
- Wear waterproof shoes or boots if you plan to hike beyond the boardwalk.
- Dawn and dusk are the best times for wildlife and bird activity on the boardwalk.
- The park hosts free ranger-led programs including canoe tours — check the schedule at the visitor center.
- Watch for cottonmouth snakes, especially near water — they are not aggressive but should not be approached.
