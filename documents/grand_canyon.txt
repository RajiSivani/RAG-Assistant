Grand Canyon National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Grand Canyon National Park is located in northwestern Arizona and was established as a national park on February 26, 1919. The Grand Canyon itself is 277 miles long, up to 18 miles wide, and over a mile deep (6,093 feet at its deepest point). The canyon was carved over millions of years by the Colorado River and exposes nearly two billion years of Earth's geological history in its layered rock walls. It is one of the Seven Natural Wonders of the World and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Location and Access:
The park is divided into two main sections: the South Rim and the North Rim. The South Rim is open year-round and receives 90% of all visitors. It is located 80 miles north of Williams, Arizona and 60 miles north of Flagstaff. The North Rim is more remote, open mid-May through mid-October, and receives far fewer visitors. The nearest major airport is Flagstaff Pulliam Airport (80 miles from the South Rim). Las Vegas (280 miles) and Phoenix (230 miles) are popular starting points for road trips.

Entry Fees:
Private vehicle: $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days.
Motorcycle: $30, valid for 7 days.
Individual on foot or bicycle: $20, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 — highly recommended if visiting multiple parks.

Best Time to Visit:
Spring (March to May) is ideal — comfortable temperatures on the rim (50-70°F), fewer crowds than summer, and occasional wildflowers. Summer (June to August) is peak season but extremely hot at the bottom of the canyon (can exceed 110°F). Rim temperatures are more moderate at 80-90°F. Fall (September to November) is excellent — comfortable temperatures, thinner crowds, and beautiful light for photography. Winter (December to February) brings snow to the rim, creating stunning scenery. The South Rim remains open but some facilities close.

Top Attractions:
1. Mather Point: The most visited viewpoint in the park, offering a sweeping panorama of the canyon. Located just steps from the visitor center — perfect first stop.
2. Bright Angel Trail: The most popular trail into the canyon, beginning at the South Rim Village. A well-maintained trail with water stations and rest houses — the only trail with water available year-round.
3. South Kaibab Trail: Offers the most dramatic ridge-top views of any trail in the canyon. No water available — carry at least 4 liters.
4. Desert View Watchtower: Located at the east end of Desert View Drive, this 70-foot stone tower offers views of the Painted Desert, Colorado River, and canyon stretching for miles.
5. Horseshoe Bend: Located just outside the park near Page, Arizona, this dramatic U-shaped bend in the Colorado River is one of the most photographed spots in the American Southwest.
6. Phantom Ranch: The only lodge located at the bottom of the canyon, on the banks of the Colorado River. Accessible only by hiking, mule, or rafting. Book reservations up to 15 months in advance.
7. Grand Canyon Skywalk: Located on the Hualapai Reservation (separate from the national park), this glass horseshoe-shaped bridge extends 70 feet over the canyon rim.
8. Hermit Road: A 7-mile scenic drive along the South Rim (private vehicles restricted — use free shuttle bus) offering 9 stunning overlooks including Pima Point and Hermit's Rest.

Wildlife:
The Grand Canyon supports diverse wildlife across its multiple ecosystems. Animals include California condors (reintroduced in 1996, now 80+ flying free), mule deer, elk, mountain lions, bobcats, ringtail cats, coyotes, ravens, Kaibab squirrels (found only on the North Rim), rattlesnakes, and Gila monsters. The Colorado River supports native fish including humpback chub.

Top Hiking Trails:
1. Rim Trail — 13 miles, easy, flat walk along the South Rim with multiple viewpoints.
2. Bright Angel Trail to 1.5-Mile Resthouse — 3 miles round trip, moderate.
3. Bright Angel Trail to Indian Garden — 9.6 miles round trip, strenuous.
4. South Kaibab to Ooh Aah Point — 1.8 miles round trip, moderate, outstanding views.
5. South Kaibab to Cedar Ridge — 3 miles round trip, moderate-strenuous.
6. Rim to Rim (South Kaibab to North Kaibab) — 21 miles one way, extremely strenuous, requires overnight stay.
WARNING: Do not attempt to hike to the river and back in one day. More people require emergency rescue on this canyon than any other in the world due to heat exhaustion and dehydration.

Camping:
Mather Campground (South Rim, 319 sites) accepts reservations up to 6 months in advance at recreation.gov. Desert View Campground (50 sites) is first-come, first-served. Bright Angel Campground (at the bottom of the canyon) requires a backcountry permit — incredibly popular and competitive to obtain.

Where to Stay:
El Tovar Hotel is the historic grand lodge perched on the South Rim — one of the most iconic hotels in the national park system, built in 1905. Bright Angel Lodge, Maswik Lodge, and Yavapai Lodge offer more affordable in-park options. All are managed by Xanterra and must be booked well in advance.

Tips for Visitors:
- Never hike below the rim in summer afternoon heat — most rescues happen between noon and 4pm.
- Carry and drink at least half a liter of water per hour while hiking.
- The South Rim free shuttle system covers most key viewpoints — use it to avoid parking stress.
- Sunrise at Mather Point and sunset at Hopi Point are unmissable experiences.
- Cell service is spotty — download the NPS Grand Canyon app with offline maps before arriving.
- Mule rides to the bottom are extremely popular — book a year in advance.
