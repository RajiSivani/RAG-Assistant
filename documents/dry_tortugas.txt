Dry Tortugas National Park - Complete Guide

Overview:
Dry Tortugas National Park is located approximately 70 miles west of Key West, Florida, in the Gulf of Mexico, and was established as a national park on October 26, 1992 (previously a national monument since 1935). It covers 101 square miles (261 sq km), of which 99% is open water. The park consists of seven small coral islands and their surrounding waters, and is accessible only by boat or seaplane. The centerpiece is Fort Jefferson — the largest 19th-century coastal fort in the United States and one of the most isolated — built on Garden Key starting in 1846. The park is known for its brilliant blue waters, spectacular coral reefs, abundant marine life, and as a critical nesting site for sea birds. It is one of the most remote and least-visited national parks.

Location and Access:
The park is located 70 miles west of Key West, accessible only by ferry or seaplane. The Yankee Freedom III is the primary ferry service operating from Key West's Historic Seaport — a 2.5-hour crossing. Seaplanes of the Keys offer 10-minute flights from Key West. Key West International Airport is the nearest commercial airport. A round trip ferry costs approximately $200 per person; seaplane rates are higher. There are no cars — the park is explored entirely on foot and by water.

Entry Fees:
Ferry or seaplane transportation is required (private boat access is also possible).
Park entrance fee: $15 per person, valid for 7 days.
America the Beautiful Annual Pass: $80 (covers entrance fee).
Note: Ferry transportation is a separate cost (~$200 per person round trip).

Best Time to Visit:
Winter and Spring (November to April) are ideal — calm seas, comfortable temperatures (70-80°F), excellent visibility for snorkeling, and the lowest chance of hurricanes. The sooty tern and brown noddy nesting season (January/February to September) brings enormous bird colonies to Bush Key. May through June is the prime sea turtle nesting season. Summer (June to September) is hurricane season with rough weather possible; the park may close during major storms. September and October have the highest hurricane risk.

Top Attractions:
1. Fort Jefferson: A massive hexagonal brick fort with 16 million bricks, designed to control navigation through the Gulf. Never completed (construction began in 1846 and continued for 30 years) and never fired a shot in war, it was used as a prison — most famously for Dr. Samuel Mudd, who was convicted of conspiracy in Lincoln's assassination. Tours are self-guided; the moat and walls are explorable.
2. Snorkeling the Moat and Reef: The moat wall surrounding Fort Jefferson harbors an exceptional variety of coral, fish, and sea life — barracuda, colorful reef fish, sea turtles, and nurse sharks are commonly seen. The Loggerhead Reef just offshore offers more advanced snorkeling.
3. Bush Key Bird Colony: From January through September, approximately 80,000 sooty terns and 3,000 brown noddies nest on Bush Key adjacent to Garden Key — one of the largest such colonies in the Caribbean region. The noise and spectacle are extraordinary.
4. Sea Turtle Nesting: The beaches of the Tortugas are important nesting sites for loggerhead, green, and leatherhead sea turtles. From May through August, nesting activity can be observed (at a respectful distance) after dark.
5. Star Gazing and Sunsets: The extreme isolation — 70 miles from city lights — provides some of the darkest skies on the East Coast. The sunsets over the Gulf are spectacular.
6. Sailing and Snorkeling Day Trip: Many visitors arrive by catamaran ferry which includes breakfast, lunch, and snorkeling gear — a full day trip from Key West.

Wildlife:
The surrounding waters host loggerhead, green, and hawksbill sea turtles, bottlenose dolphins, nurse sharks, bull sharks, spotted eagle rays, and hundreds of fish species. The coral reefs support over 442 fish species and 31 coral species. The bird colonies on Bush Key include sooty terns and brown noddies in vast numbers; magnificent frigatebirds, masked boobies, and peregrine falcons are also present. The park sits on the historic flyway for migratory birds — over 300 species have been recorded.

Top Activities:
1. Snorkeling — The moat wall and nearby reefs offer excellent snorkeling; gear available from ferry.
2. Exploring Fort Jefferson — Self-guided tour of this massive, remarkably preserved Civil War era fort.
3. Birdwatching — Exceptional during the tern nesting season (February-September).
4. Camping — Primitive overnight camping under the stars on Garden Key.
5. Fishing — Excellent saltwater fishing in surrounding waters; Florida fishing license required.
6. Scuba Diving — The Windjammer wreck, a 19th-century sailing ship, is a popular dive site.

Camping:
Garden Key Campground has 10 primitive campsites on the beach, first-come, first-served. Reservations can be made at recreation.gov. There is no fresh water on the island — all water must be brought from the mainland. Campers must bring everything they need and pack out all trash. The ferry can transport camping gear. Staying overnight allows for sunrise over the fort, the full dark sky at night, and the experience of having the island largely to yourself.

Where to Stay:
There is no lodging in the park. Virtually all visitors stay in Key West, which offers hotels at every price point. Camping in the park is the only on-site option.

Tips for Visitors:
- Book ferry tickets well in advance — the Yankee Freedom III fills up, especially in winter and spring.
- Bring all food, water, and supplies for the day — facilities on the island are minimal.
- Seasickness medication is recommended — the ferry crossing can be rough.
- Snorkeling gear is provided on the ferry but bringing your own ensures proper fit.
- The park is off-limits to casual visitors during nesting season on Bush Key — respect the restrictions.
- Sunscreen must be reef-safe — chemical sunscreens harm the coral.
- The combination of remote location, history, wildlife, and natural beauty makes this one of the most extraordinary day trips in America.
